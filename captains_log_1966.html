Sat 24 Sep 1966 🧢 1st XIv HC Hartley Country Club CC · HomeformLWLWW

Saturday 24th September, and we welcomed Hartley Country Club to Linton Park under skies that felt, for once, in a benevolent mood. I won the toss and had no hesitation in taking first strike — the right call, and I'll take the credit while it's going.

168/9 from our 44 overs told a story of feast, famine, and a defiant final flourish. Dick Langridge batted with real composure for 46 not out, well backed by Terry Whittaker's 32 and Derry Evans's unbeaten 27. But cricket, cruel game that it is, saw us slide from 11-1 to 12-4 before we'd barely settled — John Thirkell, Benny Bell and Mike Iliffe all back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers. Credit to the last pair, who conjured 59 priceless runs from nowhere to give the total some dignity.

In the field we held our nerve. Gordon Fenner was the chief tormentor with 3-20, Dick Langridge (3-34, five miserly maidens) barely let anyone breathe, and Paul Bowles chipped in with 1-40. E Cherry's 48 and A Lush's 24 kept Hartley in it, but they could only reach 128/7 — C Cooper falling for a duck at our hands. As for your correspondent, I too made a duck — the less said, the better.

Two names on the honours board: Terry Whittaker climbed to 15th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, and Dick Langridge to 13th among the wicket-takers. A draw, then — honours shared, and honestly, a fair reflection of a match that gave as good as it got.

Sun 18 Sep 1966 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformDLWLW

Sunday 18 September 1966, and Anchorians CC arrived as our visitors with the sun doing its level best and the toss doing its level worst — down we went, and out we were sent to bowl.

Not that any of us minded overmuch once Terry Whittaker got his fingers round the ball. Turning it square all afternoon, he finished with a scarcely believable 7 for 29, and by the time he'd finished tormenting the Anchorians middle order there wasn't much of an innings left to torment. From 3 for 1 we had them reeling to 16 for 4, A Hart and M- Brown both back in the pavilion without so much as a squint at the scoreboard, and Gordon Fenner strangling the other end with nine maidens and 3 for 37 for good measure. Only J- Lock, unbeaten on 29, kept the innings from complete collapse, and 79 all out in 37.4 overs felt, frankly, generous of us to allow.

Chasing so modest a total ought to be simple, and for once it very nearly was. Ron Wratten batted with the calm of a man who knew the hard work was already done, seeing us home unbeaten on 35, with Robert Chance (21 not out) and Stan Wratten (20) doing the early legwork. We reached 80 for 1 in 32.5 overs without ever looking troubled.

A fine win by 9 wickets, then, and no false modesty required this week — Whittaker was magnificent, Fenner was miserly, and the rest of us simply had to turn up. Onwards.

Sat 17 Sep 1966 🧢 1st XIv BC Bowaters CC · AwayformDDLWL

We travelled to Bowaters on the 17th of September full of the usual optimism, which lasted right up until I lost the toss and was sent into the field — never my favourite way to spend a Saturday, but there we are. The lads rallied well behind the ball, restricting the hosts to 129/9 from their 52 overs. Brian Relf was the pick of the attack, cutting through with figures of 3-12, while Dick Langridge (2-16) and Benny Bell (2-38) chipped in handsomely, and Terry Whittaker bowled a spell of admirable parsimony, five maidens and barely a run to be had off him.

A gettable total, then, though you wouldn't have known it from our start — 1-1 had the tea urn muttering before it had even boiled. But John Thirkell and Basil Sears steadied the ship with a stand of 61 for the second wicket, Thirkell top-scoring with a fine 38 and Sears a very useful 29. I contributed my own 38 down the order, and between us we saw the side home at 130/5 in 37.1 overs. Spare a thought for poor Benny Bell, whose morning with the ball was rather undone by an afternoon duck — the Duck Club ledger grows heavier still.

Elsewhere, Thirkell continues to lord it over the season's run charts with 932 to his name, and Langridge sits fourth among the wicket-takers with 38. A five-wicket win, two points banked, and a thoroughly satisfactory way to spend a September Saturday — even if I never did get to choose whether we batted or bowled.

Sun 11 Sep 1966 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformDDDLW

From the captain's chair — Sunday 11 September 1966. We travelled down to Staplehurst in good spirits, and I won the toss and elected to bat, which I still maintain was the right call whatever anyone says afterwards.

We posted 181 for 8 off 48.5 overs, a total built on a fine 72 from Robert Chance, who passed his fifty with something to spare, well supported by John Thirkell's 35 and Basil Sears' 27. The second-wicket stand of 79 was the innings' backbone. Poor Frank Brattle contributed a golden duck for his troubles, and the extras column weighed in with a generous 26 — Staplehurst's bowlers evidently as wayward as our batting was assured.

In the field we couldn't quite close it out. Staplehurst reached 183 for 5 off 39 overs, L- Murrell leading the chase with a well-made 71 not out, backed by G- Murrell's 39 and W Tipples' 29, their fourth-wicket stand of 61 doing the damage. Gordon Fenner bowled tidily for 2 for 31 with two maidens, Dick Langridge took 2 for 46, and Paul Bowles 1 for 39 — honest toil, but not quite enough, with Benny Bell enduring a tricky afternoon behind the stumps as 10 byes went astray. I chipped in with 35 myself for what it was worth.

There was a silver lining amid the gloom: Dick Langridge's two wickets carried him past Herbert Rayner into 14th on the club's all-time list, while John Thirkell continues to lead our 1966 run charts and Langridge sits fourth among the season's wicket-takers.

Beaten by five wickets in the end. We'll be better for it next week.

Sat 10 Sep 1966 🧢 1st XIv RC Reeds CC · HomeformLDDDL

Lost the toss on a bright September Saturday, and duly found myself condemned to the field while Reeds CC helped themselves to batting first — the fate of a captain who has never once called correctly, and probably never will. Still, we made the best of it. Jepson gave us a proper working-over with a classy 51, well backed by Dampier's 37, and their third-wicket pair put on a stubborn 57 that had me wondering whether we'd be there till Sunday. But cricket has a way of turning on its head: from 101 for 4 they were 108 for 7 before anyone had finished their tea, Medhurst, Holmes and Franks all trudging back for ducks. Dick Langridge was the chief tormentor, a wonderfully mean 5 for 18 with two maidens thrown in for good measure, Paul Bowles chipping in with 2 for 45 and Terry Whittaker with 1 for 27. All out 122 in 37.5 overs — a total we could work with.

And work with it we did. A second-wicket stand of 62 set us on our way, though poor Benny Bell will want to forget his golden duck in a hurry. As for yours truly, I'm rather pleased to report I led from the front with 79 not out, seeing us home at 123 for 4 in 37.4 overs.

Six wickets the margin, and a thoroughly good day for the club. Dick's bowling and my own modest run of form this season — now the top of the pile with the ball and bat respectively — suggest 1966 still has a few more Saturdays like this in store.

I managed a tidy 48 myself, more by luck than judgement.

A 35-run victory, then, and a thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work all round. With Thirkell topping the run charts and Ames the wicket column for the season, the Midweek XI is looking in fine fettle. Onwards indeed.

Honours shared, then — a draw when neither side could quite land the killer blow. Frustrating, perhaps, but with Thirkell topping our run charts and Langridge lurking fifth among the wicket-takers, there's no cause for gloom in the Linton Park dressing room.
