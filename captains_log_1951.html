🗓️ 29 Sep – 05 Oct 1951 · 1951 — 2 matches

Sun 30 Sep 1951 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · Homeform—

Sunday 30th September, and we welcomed Linton Park CC for what I'd hoped would be a season-defining afternoon. The coin obliged, and with a rare glint of tactical confidence I put us in — a decision I stand by, whatever the scorebook may later suggest.

We were bowled out for 183 in 58.5 overs, which sounds tidier on paper than it felt in the deckchairs. Basil Sears led the way with a handsome 66, and Frank Taylor chipped in a well-crafted 50, the two of them stitching together a 92-run second-wicket stand that was, frankly, the only thing holding the innings together with any dignity. Robert Muir's 34 added further respectability. Elsewhere, the less said the better — Don Hartridge, Dennis Smith and H Newport all departed for ducks, a trio of blobs that rather undid the good work of their colleagues.

There was at least a moment of quiet history amid the wreckage: Frank Taylor's fifty nudged him past Herbert Rayner and into 6th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — a nice line for the archives, even on a losing day. Basil Sears also tops our 1951 Married run charts, with Ray Tippen leading the Singles.

We went down by 128 runs in the end — no dressing it up. Heads up, chins high, and back to the nets we go.

🗓️ 22 Sep – 28 Sep 1951 · 1951 — 1 match

Sat 22 Sep 1951 🧢 1st XIv HC Hunton Wanderers CC · AwayformWLDDD

Lost the toss at Hunton on Saturday, and duly found ourselves leather in hand rather than willow — never my favourite way to spend a Saturday, but there it is. Their openers made themselves thoroughly at home, putting on 76 before we prised the first one out, and the innings settled into A. Pilcher's hands, 90 not out and forever just out of our reach, with Reg Oldfield's 30 lending a hand. Gordon Fenner did the damage that mattered, 2-27, with Robert Muir (1-12) and Frank Taylor (1-62) chipping in, while John --'s Taylor bowled six maidens and gave the scorers precious little to do. They closed on 160/4 from 46 overs — a fair total, and no more than that.

Chasing it, we lost an early wicket with only 3 on the board, which is never the tonic one wants, and John --'s Taylor followed his miserly bowling with a golden duck at the other end 🦆 — the sort of symmetry cricket delights in inflicting. Nine byes told their own tale of a tricky day behind the stumps for poor Bob Arnold. As for your captain, I contributed precisely nought and shall say no more about it. But Frank Taylor stood firm for 66 not out to see us to 90/3 from 31 overs — and in doing so brought up 1,000 career runs for the club 🎉, a landmark thoroughly deserved from a man who also leads our 1951 wicket charts with 90, while William Buck sits third in the run charts on 621.

A draw, then — honours shared, and honestly, about right.

Sun 16 Sep 1951 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWLDD

Sunday 16th September, and Dummy Site kind enough to make the trip to us. Won the toss, and there really was no debate in my mind — on it looked, and bat we would.

We made it to 245 for 6 off our 67 overs, a total I was rather proud of by the close. John --'s Taylor led the way with 43, William Buck weighed in with 40, and Bill Hall stood firm at 40 not out — the openers laying a fine platform of 56, and then, wonderfully, a last-wicket pair who clearly hadn't read the script adding a defiant 56 of their own to give the innings a bit of a shine it barely deserved.

With the ball, we were rampant. Frank Taylor was quite simply magnificent, tearing through them with 5 for 21 and two maidens to boot, with Bill Hall chipping in at 2 for 36. Dummy Site stumbled early, lost their nerve in the middle, and collapsed from 90 for 5 to 102 for 8 in the blink of an eye, D- MacDonald's 37 and C- MacDonald's 22 the only real resistance. They limped to 106 for 8 off 39 overs before we ran out of time to finish the job.

A draw, then, honours shared — though not before Taylor moved to 4th on the club's all-time run charts and Frank Taylor climbed to 2nd among our wicket-takers. Not a bad afternoon's work, even if I say so myself.

A word, too, for Don Hartridge, whose 23 today eased him past Basil Sears into 12th on the club's all-time run-charts — quiet history in the making. With John --'s Taylor still leading the 1951 runs (811) and Frank Taylor the wickets (84), the old firm remains in good order.

Neither side could force it home, so honours were shared. A draw, then — not the result either captain wanted, but an honest one, and I'll settle for that with a personal 59 in the bank.

And with 683 runs and 82 wickets this season respectively, John Taylor and Frank Taylor continue to make the rest of us look distinctly amateur. A thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work, and one we shall be dining out on all week.
