🗓️ 27 Sep – 03 Oct 1958 · 1958 — 2 matches

Sun 28 Sep 1958 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformDDLDL

Sunday 28 September 1958 dawned fair, and with the coin obliging me for once, I did what every captain dreams of and rather too few can pull off with a straight face — I put us in. Bold, some might say; foolhardy, others may yet judge, once they've read on.

Clive Smith was the innings, no argument there — 76 not out, a proper well-made fifty and then some, carrying us with the sort of application the rest of the order might usefully have studied. We closed on 156 for 9 from our 47, which felt, even as the last over ticked away, like a total assembled more by graft than glory. Spare a thought for Tony Levick, whose duck joins the club ledger for posterity — the long, lonely walk back that every one of us has made at some point, usually when least expecting it. On a brighter note, Tom Osborn's five runs were quietly historic, nudging him past Don Hartridge into 11th on the all-time run-scorers list — the sort of landmark that means everything to the man himself and precisely nothing to the scoreboard.

The season's ledgers roll on regardless: Stan Wratten sits 4th in the 1958 1st XI charts on 394, Clive leads the Married averages, and Terry Whittaker tops the Singles — bragging rights, at least, secure for the tea interval.

As for the match itself, Linton Park had the better of us by 94 runs in the end, a margin that rather flatters the closeness of the contest. We'll be better for it next week — we usually are, at least in theory.

Skipper's Log — Saturday 27 September 1958

Won the toss and, in a fit of what I told myself was tactical boldness, stuck us in. Ninety-two all out from 44.3 overs is not a total you build a war memorial around, and with Terry Whittaker and Tony Snell both departing for ducks, there were long stretches where our scoring looked positively theoretical. Still, the lads dug in where they could, and we walked off feeling we'd at least given the bowlers something, however modest, to defend.

And defend it we did. Their reply folded for 67 in 24.1 overs, with only H. Clancy offering any real resistance for 27, and a. Trembeth, K. McDowall and J. Mortlock all sent back without so much as a scoreboard blush. The afternoon belonged entirely to M. Harden, whose 7-18 — five maidens of it — was bowling of the sort you talk about in the clubhouse for years rather than weeks. Ray Howard chipped in nobly with 3-29, and it's worth noting his was a day of quiet history too: those 14 runs and 3 wickets nudged him past Bob Arnold and the Rev Thomas Burra into 14th and 20th on the club's all-time lists respectively.

And yet we lost, by 25 runs — proof, if any were needed, that even a spell for the ages can't fully rescue a batting card as threadbare as ours. Not our day. Heads up, and back to the nets.

1958 was a long time ago, but a match report doesn't need current tools — I'll write it straight from the facts given.

Winning the toss on a fine Sunday, I took one look at the strip and decided we'd bat — a decision I spent much of the afternoon quietly reconsidering. Seventy-nine all out in thirty-four overs and two balls is not a total to frighten anyone, and the scorebook makes for grim reading in places: Stan Wratten, John Ames and Roy Clarke all back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers, ducks all round. John Ames, so profligate with the bat, turned magician with the ball — 5 for 26 of pure spin wizardry that undid batsman after batsman. Alan Wilkes was hardly a passenger either, snaring 5 for 33 with two miserly maidens thrown in, and G. Winters, R. Bailey and T. Maskell all departed for ducks of their own.

For all that, twenty runs proved beyond us in the end, and I've no complaints — 79 was never enough, however well the ball was bowled. John Thirkell continues to lead our run charts for the season with 655, and John Ames now sits proudly atop the wicket-takers with 59. We were beaten, fair and square, but there's a side in there worth persisting with — we'll be better for it next week.

Sunday 14th September saw us motor over to Tunbridge Wells CC with hopes reasonably high and the coin, as ever, entirely indifferent to them. All out for 77 in 28.2 overs, we were beaten by 98 runs and never remotely in it.

Small mercies: John Thirkell continues to lead our run charts with 650 for the season, and John Ames, duck notwithstanding, tops the wicket-takers with 54. As for the rest of us — well, the tea was good, the drive home was quiet, and we'll be considerably better for it next week.

In the end we fell two wickets short, and honestly, with a total like ours, we were fortunate it was that close. Heads up, back to the nets — there is bowling to be proud of here, and batting very much to be worked on.
