Wed 28 Sep 1983 🧢 Midweek XIv Penshurst Park CC · AwayformWDDDL

Wednesday the 28th of September found us on the road to Penshurst Park, and having called correctly at the toss I didn't dither over the decision — a good morning for batting if ever I saw one, and I wasn't about to let it go to waste. We settled in nicely, running up 211 for 3 from our 44 overs. I'll confess to a quiet satisfaction in leading from the front with 78, though Andy Woollett was hardly a passenger, cracking a fine 58 past his own fifty, while Bob Lines saw us home in style with an unbeaten 36.

With the ball, things went rather better still. Penshurst fought hard and posted a respectable 180 for 9 off their 45, Richard Watters top-scoring with a well-crafted 54 and N Fowler chipping in 46, but poor Jim Littlechild trudged back without troubling the scorers. I found some turn to spin through them for 5 for 60 — and, unlikely as it sounds, that same over of business saw me tick past 900 career wickets, which is very much one for the mantelpiece. Trevor Back and Malcolm Pay lent able support, and Michael Chambers bowled with the meanness of a man guarding his allotment, five maidens and all.

In the end neither side could force it, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but with a milestone in the bag and the season's charts still mine to lead, I'll take that as a very good afternoon's work.

Sun 25 Sep 1983 🧢 1st XIv Linton Park CC · HomeformDDDWW

Skipper's Log — Sunday 25 September 1983

I'll confess I fancied our chances the moment that coin landed my way, and, with the pitch looking a shade too kind to leave out, I had few qualms about batting first. Malcolm Pay set about proving me right with a fine 45, Brian Piper stood firm for an unbeaten 43, and Bob Lines chipped in with a tidy 35, the three of them dragging us to a very respectable 202 all out from our 45 overs — though I use "respectable" loosely, given poor Jim Hall's afternoon began and ended without troubling the scorers. Even the extras got in on the act with a generous 29, rather more than some of the specialist batsmen managed, and I added a workmanlike 20 of my own, more grit than grace.

There was a proper occasion to mark, too: Mike Iliffe brought up his 300th appearance in Linton colours, a milestone that rather put the rest of us whippersnappers in our place and deserved a good deal more than the ripple of applause it got between overs.

As for the season at large, Paul Bowles continues to purr along in 2nd spot in the 1st XI charts with 996 runs to his name, while over in the 2nds Tim Thirkell leads the way with 51.

In the end it came down to the finest of margins — beaten by just 2 runs, a result that will sting rather more for its closeness than any thumping ever could. We'll be better for it next week.

🗓️ 17 Sep – 23 Sep 1983 · 1983 — 1 match

Sat 17 Sep 1983 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · AwayformDDDDW

As Mrs. Higgins in the tea hut once observed, you can tell a good deal about a captain's judgement by what he does with the coin, and on this occasion I'd like to think mine was sound. Winning the toss at West Farleigh and electing to bat felt, even at the time, like rather a shrewd move — and by the close of our innings I was struggling to disagree with myself. Nigel Thirkell played an innings of real substance, 107 of the finest, and found a willing ally in Jim Hall, whose well-constructed 73 kept the scoreboard humming along nicely. Between them they steered us to 201 for 3 off our 39 overs, a total that had a satisfying heft to it as we trooped off for tea.

Defending it was, happily, a rather more comfortable afternoon than some I could mention. Paul Bowles was in irresistible form, snaring five for 27 and bowling with the kind of relentless accuracy that has strangers in the crowd asking his name, while Jon Harris chipped in handsomely with five for 40 of his own — between the two of them there wasn't much West Farleigh could do. Mike Elworthy, meanwhile, went about his business with three tidy maidens, the sort of unglamorous graft that wins matches without ever making the highlights. Peter Cooper, Tony Hyde and Micky Back all got in but couldn't kick on, and poor Fred Baker and Don Fermor departed without so much as troubling the scorer — these things happen to the best of us. All out for 122 in 38.1 overs told its own story.

A win by 79 runs, then, and a thoroughly satisfying one. Onwards.
