Sun 27 Sep 1981 🧢 1st XIv Linton Park CC · HomeformDWLWD

Cricket returned to Linton Park on Sunday, and away from home we went, with the toss falling kindly and me deciding we should bat first — the right call, I still maintain, whatever the scoreboard later suggested. The lads set about their work with reasonable purpose, Malcolm Pay leading the way with a fine 32, Jon Harris chipping in 29 and Martin Mears adding a tidy 27, while I contributed a modest 22 of my own before the innings closed on 159 for 7 from our 24 overs — a total that felt like it had something in it, if not quite enough.

There was, mind you, rather more to celebrate than the runs alone. Jon Harris brought up 3,000 career runs for the club in that innings of 29, a landmark achieved with typically little fuss, and in doing so eased past Terry Whittaker into 16th on our all-time run-scorers list. Elsewhere in the season's ledger, Nigel Thirkell continues to plunder runs at the top of the 1st XI table with 1,661 to his name, while Gareth Thomas leads the 2nd XI charge with 35.

Sadly the afternoon belonged to our opponents, who chased down what we'd set with seven wickets in hand and precious little alarm. Not our day, then — heads up, chins high, and back to the nets we go.

So honours shared, a draw when neither side could quite force the issue — and with Jim Hall still leading our run charts and Jon Harris now sitting on 20 wickets for the season, there's plenty still to play for. A fair result, fairly earned, and the tea was rather good too.

Wednesday, 09 September saw us take the road to Penryn St Gluvias, and winning the toss I did not hesitate to bat first — the pitch had "runs" written all over it, and I like to think I can still read a wicket even if the knees no longer read the singles quite so eagerly. Jaymin Patel's 21 was the only real resistance as the home side folded for 88 in 31 overs, Bettison and Hocking both departing without troubling the scorer.

Paul Bowles had a busier afternoon behind the stumps than his 19 byes suggest he'd have liked, but nobody will remember that once they hear Michael Chambers passed 100 career wickets in the same match — a landmark thoroughly deserved and warmly toasted in the bar. A 122-run victory, two points banked, and, dare I say it, rather a good day to be captain.
