Sun 23 Sep 1962 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLLLDD

Sunday 23rd September brought Linton Park CC to our ground, and having called correctly at the toss I did what any captain raised on the primacy of runs on the board would do — I stuck us in to bat. Confidence, I confess, was riding rather higher than it had any right to.

What followed will not trouble the history books. Ten for three from two overs is not so much a batting collapse as a batting implosion, the sort of scoreline that has you double-checking the scorebook and wondering whether the umpire miscounted the overs. Three wickets down before the openers had so much as loosened their shoulders properly — one struggles to recall a briefer, more chastening spell at the crease. I shall take my share of the blame for the decision at the toss, though in fairness no amount of captaincy wisdom accounts for what unfolded once bat met ball.

And yet cricket, bless it, has a way of levelling things out. For all our early troubles, honours finished shared — neither side able to force home the advantage, and a draw was the fair and honest result of an afternoon that promised drama and rather delivered farce.

So: a match to file under "character-building" rather than "vintage". We batted like strangers to the game, but we held our nerve to share the spoils, and for that alone the lads deserve a pint at the bar.

William Buck brought up 5,000 career runs for the club, a landmark achieved with typical lack of fuss, while Roy Clarke's watchful 17 eased him past William Blight and into 25th on our all-time run-scorers' list — the sort of statistical nudge that means everything to those of us who pore over the club averages come winter. In the end neither side could force the issue, and honours were shared. A draw, then — not the stuff of legend, but a thoroughly pleasant Sunday's cricket, and I'll happily take that.

Sun 16 Sep 1962 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformLLWDW

Won the toss at Staplehurst, called correctly, and never seriously considered doing anything but batting first — old habits, and all that. We laboured to 153/8 from our 43.3 overs, which sounds a modest total until you remember the shape it came in. Ray Howard's 57 was the innings' backbone, a proper half-century, with Stan Wratten's 28 and John Thirkell's 25 adding the flesh to the bones and a second-wicket stand of 68 doing the heavy lifting. Then, as is our club's wont, we contrived to turn 146-4 into 152-7 in what felt like the time it takes to pour a tea. Richard Ashby and Brian Relf both departed without troubling the scorer — ducks the pair of them, bless them.

With ball in hand we were a different side altogether. Staplehurst were bowled out for a mere 64 in 24.3 overs, D Thirkell's defiant 25 the only real resistance, and once they slipped from 48-2 to 58-5 the innings had the look of a house of cards in a draught. Ray Howard was magnificent, 5-20 and thoroughly unplayable, with Alan Tucker's miserly 4-1 — four maidens included — squeezing the life out of them, and David Gale chipping in with 1-25. K Hyde, R Thirkell and T Morris all bagged ducks of their own for good measure. I even found 25 runs myself, which I shall not let anyone forget in a hurry.

A grand day too for the record books — Roy Clarke reached 1,000 career runs for the club, and Alan Tucker's four wickets lifted him past Dennis Smith to 21st on our all-time list. Won by 89 runs: two points banked, and thoroughly, unarguably earned.
