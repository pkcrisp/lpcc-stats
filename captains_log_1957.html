Sun 29 Sep 1957 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformDDDLD

Sunday last brought Linton Park CC out for a home fixture, and for once the coin obliged me — I called correctly and, in a fit of what I can only describe as misplaced confidence, put us in to bat. It did not, in hindsight, cover me in glory.

Sixty-four all out in 27.2 overs is not the sort of total that has opposition captains reaching for the record books, unless it's the record for shortest tea interval required. Tony Levick was the exception to an otherwise grim afternoon, top-scoring with 27 and looking, briefly, like a man playing a different match to the rest of us. Stan Wratten and C. Haylor, meanwhile, both contributed golden ducks — a matching pair, like bookends, propping up absolutely nothing.

There was, I suppose, a silver lining of sorts up the season table: Tony Levick's 27 keeps him top of our 1957 Married run charts, while Clive Smith continues to lord it over the Singles column with 38. Small comfort when chasing a target that never had a chance of being defended.

We went down by 3 wickets in the end, and no amount of gallows humour in the pavilion could disguise that 64 simply wasn't cricket, in the fullest sense of the phrase. Heads up, chins slightly less so, and back to the nets — there is clearly work to be done before we trouble anyone's scorers again.

Sat 28 Sep 1957 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWLDL

Saturday 28th September 1957, and Dummy Site were our visitors — though not, as it turned out, for terribly long. I lost the toss (a coin I have never once called correctly in this life) and duly consigned us to the field, which on reflection did us rather a favour.

They crumbled to 80 all out in 30.4 overs, with only L- Fell showing any real backbone for 28. Rye, Castle and Mortlock trooped off for ducks apiece, largely thanks to John --'s Taylor, who bowled like a man possessed for 7-29 with two maidens thrown in for good measure. Ray Howard chipped in admirably with 2-10. It was, in short, the sort of spell one dines out on for years.

Batting proved a rather more nervous affair. We scraped to 82/9 from 33.3 overs, Tom Osborn leading the way with a watchful 20 — a knock that, pleasingly, also lifts him past Richard Harris into 12th on the club's all-time run charts. Ray Howard and John --'s Taylor both made the long walk back for nought, joining the Duck Club in good company, while poor William Buck had a torrid time behind the stumps, shipping nine byes. Basil Sears, meanwhile, continues his imperious season, now second in the county with 550 runs.

A win by a single wicket, then — the sort that ages a captain a decade in an afternoon, but a win is a win, and we'll take it gladly.

Sun 22 Sep 1957 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformWLWLD

1957 saw a cracking home fixture, and I still can't quite believe we made 79 look chaseable. Winning the toss and putting them in felt like the right call at the time — and for a while it was, John Taylor bowling with real venom to take five-for-fifty-one, backed up beautifully by Cliff Anscombe's four wickets for eighteen off a miserly four maidens, and Alan Wilkes chipping in for one. R- Bailey went for a duck at our hands, which cheered us no end, though T- Maskell had other ideas and carried them to 79 all out from his unbeaten twenty-five. Restricting anyone to 79 from 34 overs ought to have been the day's hard work done.

Alas, cricket has a cruel sense of humour, and ours was about to be tested. We folded for 57 in 23 overs, which even by our own modest standards was a chastening effort. John Thirkell top-scored with a gutsy twenty — no surprise there, the man's leading our 1957 run charts with 1005 for the season and showed exactly why — and I managed to scratch together twenty of my own, for whatever comfort that provides. William Buck, C- Haylor and Cliff Anscombe all made ducks, which is rather a lot of golden eggs for one afternoon, and the innings never found its feet.

Beaten by 22 runs, then, and no arguing with the arithmetic — we bowled like champions and batted like men who'd left their nerve in the pavilion. Credit to Taylor and Anscombe for a spell to remember, and to Wilkes for keeping his season's thirty-three wickets ticking along nicely. We'll be better for it next week — we rather have to be.

Sun 15 Sep 1957 🧢 1st XIv TC Tunbridge Wells CC · HomeformLDWLW

Sunday 15 September brought Tunbridge Wells CC to our ground, and having called correctly at the toss, I fancied a bat first without a moment's hesitation — a decision I stand by, whatever the scorebook says about the eventual result.

We compiled 161/6 from our 48.2 overs, built on a fine knock from John Thirkell, who sailed past his fifty to make 55, with Stan Wratten (35) and John Waters (29) chipping in handsomely around him. A respectable total, we thought, and one that gave our bowlers something to work with.

Alas, Roger Phillips had other ideas, guiding the visitors home with an unbeaten 52, well supported by Charles Wicker's 40 and Len Goldsmith's 27 not out, as Tunbridge Wells reached 162/4 in 39.4 overs for a six-wicket win. There were bright spots with the ball — John Ames toiled hard for his 2-71, I chipped in with 1-18, and Bob Brigden bowled two tidy maidens — and Brian Dunster departed without troubling the scorers, though he had plenty of company in that regard by the close.

Amid the disappointment, a proud moment: William Buck brought up 4,000 career runs for the club, a landmark worth raising a glass to regardless of the scoreline. I was pleased enough with my own 55 leading from the front, and Thirkell continues to top our 1957 run charts with 933, Ames our wickets with 49.

Not our day, in the end — beaten by six wickets. Heads up, and back to the nets.
