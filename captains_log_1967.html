🗓️ 30 Sep – 06 Oct 1967 · 1967 — 1 match

Sat 30 Sep 1967 🧢 1st XIv Headcorn CC · AwayformWLLDD

Saturday last, the road took us to Headcorn, and the toss – as is traditional – went against us. They batted first, and R- Excell and C- Smith set about building an innings that would have been a good deal more troublesome had we not had Terry Donovan steaming in. A 99-run stand for the second wicket threatened to bat us clean out of the game, Excell reaching a fine 67 and Smith following him past his own fifty with 58, before Donovan (3-26, five maidens and not a run given away lightly) and Terry Whittaker (2-45) hauled things back. 145/5 from their 46 overs felt like a total we could chase, if only we could find the legs for it.

Chase it we did, after a fashion. John Thirkell led the way with a watchful 39, well supported by Basil Sears' 23 and Alan Cridland's unbeaten 23, and I contributed 39 of my own for what it's worth. We closed on 113/5 from our 35, honours and the game shared as neither side could quite land the decisive blow.

A word for the season so far – Thirkell now sits atop our 1967 run charts with 681, and Donovan lies second among the wicket-takers with 48, proof enough that Saturday's near-miss was no fluke but part of a fine campaign. A draw, then, and no shame in it – Headcorn will have gone home relieved, and so, in truth, did we.

🗓️ 23 Sep – 29 Sep 1967 · 1967 — 1 match

Sat 23 Sep 1967 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · HomeformLWLLD

Terry Whittaker set the tone with a well-made fifty, 56 of the finest, and found good company in Paul Bowles (27) and John Thirkell (22) as we recovered from the loss of an early wicket at 7 to post 146 for 7 from our 48 overs — the second-wicket pair adding a crucial 75 to steady the ship. Basil Sears and Malcolm Pay, it must be said, will not trouble the scorers of this report beyond the ornithological, each departing for a golden duck. Still, a defendable total, and a fair reward for the recovery.

West Farleigh set about the chase with intent, the openers laying a handsome platform of 65 before the first wicket fell, and for a while it looked as though our total might not be quite enough. Micky Back (44) and Joe Fenner (37) did the heavy lifting, but credit to the lads for dragging them from 96 for 3 to 102 for 6 when the game was there to be won — Brian Relf leading the charge with a fine 3 for 21, well supported by Jon Harris (2 for 13) and John Thirkell, who chipped in both with bat and ball (1 for 17) to cap another prolific afternoon on his way to 642 runs for the season. I added a modest 22 of my own, more application than artistry.

In the end West Farleigh closed on 118 for 6 from their 31, and honours were shared. Not quite the storybook finish, but a proper tussle, honestly fought, and a draw that flattered neither side nor shamed either.

🗓️ 16 Sep – 22 Sep 1967 · 1967 — 1 match

Sun 17 Sep 1967 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformLLWLL

Sunday 17 September 1967 brought Anchorians CC to our ground, and with the toss falling my way I had no hesitation in batting first — a decision I shall happily take credit for, whatever the umpires' private opinions. Openers John Thirkell (37) and John Ames (42) gave us the sort of platform that lets a captain relax on the pavilion steps, taking us to 64 before the first wicket fell, and a handsome 69-run fourth-wicket stand kept the innings moving nicely. Paul Bowles finished unbeaten on 44 to see us safely to 162 for 5 from our 47 overs — respectable enough to defend, if not quite enough to have the tea urn shaking in its boots.

And defend it we did, though not without early fireworks with the ball. C Brooker and E Batchelor were both sent back without troubling the scorers — a pair of ducks to warm any bowler's heart — as the Anchorians slumped to 7 for 1. Alan Wilkes was the architect of it all, a thoroughly miserly 9 maidens underpinning figures of 5 for 39, with Terry Whittaker chipping in a tidy 2 for 18. A Watkins (32) and P Phelan (20) did their best to drag the visitors back into contention, and I even allowed myself 37 with bat in hand earlier on, but 86 for 7 from their 44 overs left them well short of our total.

With Thirkell now heading our 1967 run charts on 620 and Wilkes leading the wickets with 63, the season's engine room is clearly in fine working order. As for Sunday's efforts — honours shared, a draw when neither side could force the issue. Not the finish we wanted, but with bowling like that, victories surely can't be far behind.

🗓️ 09 Sep – 15 Sep 1967 · 1967 — 2 matches

Sun 10 Sep 1967 🧢 1st XIv HC Harrietsham CC · HomeformLLLWL

We batted first at home to Harrietsham on a fine Sunday, and having called correctly I did what any captain with a scrap of sense does and stuck us in. It proved a sound instinct: Paul Bowles and Robert Chance gave us the platform we'd been after, seventy-two for the first wicket before the breakthrough finally came, and from there Bowles simply refused to get out, finishing unbeaten on 48. Roger Craddock chipped in with a fine 39, Chance's 34 was no less handy, and 165 for 4 off our 46 overs looked, from the pavilion at least, like a total with some substance to it. My own contribution of 17 will not trouble the historians, but every run counts on a Sunday.

Defending it, though, is quite another matter, and Harrietsham set about proving as much. Anthony Moss played the innings of the day, 74 that took the game away from us, with Keith Chaney (30) and J Wisden (25) doing their bit in a stubborn 53-run third-wicket stand. Dick Langridge toiled manfully for 3-54, and Roger Craddock, having already batted us to respectability, turned his arm over for 3-58 and two tidy maidens besides — not a man to give anything away lightly. But it wasn't quite enough, and they got home by three wickets with half an over in hand.

A fighting effort, undone by a top-order partnership we simply couldn't match. John Thirkell continues to lead our run charts handsomely and Langridge sits fourth among the wicket-takers, so the season's not short of bright spots. This one, though, goes down as a defeat we'll shrug off, dust down, and come back stronger from next Sunday.

Wednesday 6th September, and off we trundled to Birchington CC with the sun doing its best and my optimism, as ever, doing rather more work than it should. I won the toss and elected to bat — the right call, I felt, and I shall die on that hill regardless of what followed.

I contributed a duck myself. We shall move swiftly on.

With the ball we asked questions without quite getting the answers, Birchington cruising to 119/4 in 30.1 overs. L Smith's unbeaten 57 and J Darby's 34 did the damage, with a chunky third-wicket stand of 65 the difference. Terry Whittaker (1-18), Dick Langridge (1-21) and Terry Donovan (1-24) all struck — Donovan's wicket enough to leapfrog Herbert Rayner into 15th on the all-time list.

Beaten by six wickets. Not our day. Heads up, back to the nets.

A splendid win by 21 runs, and a landmark day besides — Wratten now sits second on the club's all-time run-scorers list, past Basil Sears. Onwards, in fine spirits.

Sat 02 Sep 1967 🧢 1st XIv BK Beverley CC, Kent · HomeformDWWLL

Saturday, 2nd September 1967, and the good news travelled faster than the bad: I called correctly and had few doubts about batting first on our strip.

Whether that faith was misplaced is another matter entirely. R Venner, Jon Harris and P Williams all trooped back without troubling the scorers, a hat-trick of ducks that did nobody's confidence any good. Not our finest half hour with the bat, if I'm honest.

With the ball, at least, there was pride to salvage. One bright note amid the wreckage: Terry Whittaker's watchful 12 runs eased him past Frank Taylor into 14th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — a small consolation prize on a chastening afternoon.

Beaten by nine wickets, then, and no arguments about it. Heads up, chins out, and back to the nets we go.

🗓️ 26 Aug – 01 Sep 1967 · 1967 — 3 matches

Mon 28 Aug 1967 🧢 1st XIv Bearsted CC · AwayformDDWWL

It was a Monday off to Bearsted, and the coin let us down before we'd even loosened our arms — so it was pads off, ball in hand, and hope for the best. Ian Lambert had other ideas about how our day should go, mind you not with the ball but from the other end, as their openers put on 89 before we prised the first one out. Once Dick Langridge found his rhythm there was no stopping him — six wickets, six maidens, the sort of spell that has batsmen playing forward when they should be playing back — with Alan Wilkes chipping in handsomely too, and rather more meaningfully passing 600 career wickets along the way, a landmark that deserves rather more than a passing mention in these pages. We had them 151/7 and fancied ourselves, only for the last pair to wag for an unwelcome 49, leaving 221 on the board.

Chasing it, John Ames played the innings of the day, 78 full of the sort of strokes that make the tea urn worth boiling, ably assisted by Basil Sears, whose 23 quietly nudged him past Stan Wratten into second on the club's all-time run list — no fanfare, just history being made between overs. But 140/7 became 159 all out before anyone had finished their sandwich, and ten byes gave William Buck a afternoon he'll be glad to forget.

Sixty-two runs the wrong side of the ledger, then, and heads hung a little on the coach home. Character all round, runs and wickets to be proud of individually, but as a team performance — back to the nets, lads.

Sat 26 Aug 1967 🧢 1st XIv AC Aylesford Paper Mills CC · HomeformWDDDW

Saturday 26 August 1967, and Aylesford Paper Mills CC were our visitors for the afternoon. I won the toss, elected to bat, and to my great relief the decision looked sound rather than reckless — for a while, at least.

David Keynes (26) and R Shephard (25) offered the only real resistance, but Terry Donovan was in irresistible form, spinning his way to 8 for 46 with five miserly maidens — proof, if any were needed, of why he sits second in the 1st XI's season wicket charts with 45 scalps. Terry Whittaker weighed in with 2 for 47, I added a watchful 17 with the bat earlier, and Aylesford folded for 117.

Ten runs the margin, two points banked, and thoroughly deserved — a fine day's work, bootlaces and all.
