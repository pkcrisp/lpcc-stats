🗓️ 21 Sep – 27 Sep 1968 · 1968 — 1 match

Sat 21 Sep 1968 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · HomeformWLLDW

Beaten again, but never bowed. On a bright Saturday at Linton Park I called correctly and, with more optimism than evidence, stuck West Farleigh in. The pitch had other ideas, and we crumbled to 121 all out in 41.5 overs — the first wicket gone before the scoreboard had properly woken up at 0-1. Mike Iliffe's 24 was the innings' one real oasis, while poor Frank Brattle and Derry Evans both trudged back for ducks, quacking their way into the club ledger for posterity.

Defending 121 always felt like asking the lads to carry water in a sieve, but they gave it a proper go. West Farleigh stumbled early themselves, 2-1, and for a while we dared to dream. Paul Bowles bowled his heart out for 3-39, with Terry Whittaker miserly as ever — 2-50 and three maidens, not a run given away without a fight. But Colin Clark had other plans, farming the strike to 44 not out, ushered along by Ron Kemp's unbeaten 33 and Don Fermor's 20. I chipped in with 15 with the bat but rather less with the ball, and that wretched last pair added an infuriating 80 we simply couldn't afford, seeing them home at 122-6.

Four wickets the margin, and no arguing with it. Still, John Thirkell continues to bat like a man possessed at the top of our 1968 charts with 622 runs, and Terry Whittaker's 46 wickets keep him snapping at the leader's heels. We were second best today — but there's enough here to suggest next week belongs to us.

🗓️ 14 Sep – 20 Sep 1968 · 1968 — 1 match

Sat 14 Sep 1968 🧢 1st XIv SC St Lukes Maidstone CC · HomeformDWLLD

From the captain's chair — Saturday 14 September 1968. St Lukes Maidstone CC were the visitors, and having lost the toss I found myself doing what I do best on such occasions: standing about in the field wondering where the summer's gone.

They battled to 126 all out from 47.4 overs, M. Eastman doing the bulk of the graft with 39, but once we found our rhythm the innings rather unravelled beneath them — 13-1 became 22-4 before anyone had settled into their tea. The afternoon belonged to Terry Donovan, whose 7-52 was a spell of proper old-fashioned menace and every bit deserving of the five-for. Jon Harris (1-18) and Terry Whittaker (1-27) tidied up the rest between them, Whittaker adding six maidens for good measure — miserly enough to make St Lukes' batsmen weep quietly into their gloves.

Chasing 127, we made harder work of it than the bowling perhaps deserved, limping home at 129/5 from 41.2 overs. Paul Bowles anchored things beautifully with an unbeaten 49, Fred Greenfield (23) and Jim Hall (21) lending sturdy support. Bowles's innings also nudged him past Tom Osborn into 24th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — quite the afternoon for the man, who now sits second in this season's charts with 532 runs, while Donovan leads the wicket-takers with 55.

A five-wicket win, then, earned as much with ball as bat. Onwards we go, buttons undone and spirits high.

Sun 08 Sep 1968 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformWDWLL

Sunday 8th September saw Anchorians CC visit us at Linton Park, and winning the toss I had no hesitation in taking first strike on a pitch that looked, to my admittedly optimistic eye, like it owed us a few runs.

Roger Craddock led the way with a fine 36, and he found a willing partner in Frank Brattle (34) as the pair put on 54 for the first wicket to give us the platform every captain dreams of at breakfast and rarely gets by lunch. Robert Chance chipped in with a useful 23, but from a position of some comfort at 123 for 5 we contrived, with the enthusiasm of men queuing for the tea urn, to stumble to 130 for 8 — poor Terry Donovan a spectator throughout his own duck. We limped home to 155 for 9 off our 53, a total that flattered to deceive but would, I felt, prove defendable.

And so it did, largely. Anchorians never got away from us, A. Watkins's 27 the only real resistance as they closed on 98 for 9 from their 40 overs. Paul Bowles was quite magnificent, four wickets for 27 with five overs given away for nothing, ably backed by Dick Langridge's three for 33 and a wicket apiece for the ever-reliable Craddock.

In the end honours were shared, and perhaps rightly so — a match of fine margins that neither side quite deserved to lose. A draw, then, and a fair one at that.

As the season's roving vice-captain and match reporter, here's my write-up for the gazette.

A 31-run victory, two points banked, and John Thirkell continuing to top our run charts for the summer. All told, a day where the bowlers rescued the batsmen from themselves — and I shan't complain about that arrangement one bit.
