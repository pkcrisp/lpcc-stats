Sat 01 Sep 1945 🧢 1st XIv HC Harrietsham CC · AwayformLDLLD

Saturday last took us down the road to Harrietsham, where the coin betrayed us and we were put into the field first — never our favourite way to start an afternoon.

Harrietsham made themselves at home, racking up 167 all out in 45.5 overs, R Parsons the chief architect with a fine 62 not out, well supported by A Goodhew's 26, the pair putting on a stubborn 54 for the first wicket. But we had our moments: Peter Wakefield, R Hutchins and D Jordan all took a duck at our hands, and Herbert Rayner bowled beautifully for 4-55, with Peel-Yates (2-60) and Stan Wratten (1-13) chipping in, while Gordon Fenner refused to give an inch — four maidens' worth of miserly economy.

With the bat we set off promisingly, Tom Osborn playing the innings of the day with 64, and Herbert Rayner (44) and Stan Wratten (20) lending sturdy support — 81 came for the second wicket, and for a while 167 looked gettable. Then, as is our custom, the wheels came off spectacularly, 126-2 collapsing to 146-5 before we could catch our breath, and all out for 164 in 49.2 overs. Tom's gloves had a torrid time too, 16 byes leaking through. I managed 44 with the bat and, happily, 4 wickets with the ball — small consolation on a losing day.

There was cheer amid the gloom: Stan Wratten's runs lifted him past Ernest Cruttenden into 7th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, and Herbert Rayner's four wickets took him past Jack Fryer into 2nd on the all-time wicket-takers' roll. Tom Osborn and Herbert lead the season's charts too. But three runs shy is three runs shy — not our day. Heads up, and back to the nets.

Sun 26 Aug 1945 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformWLDLL

Sunday the 26th of August, 1945, dawned fine enough for me to win the toss and, in a fit of confidence I would shortly come to regret, stick us in to bat. We were bowled out for 123 in 51.2 overs — a total that flattered to deceive. T Crawford top-scored with a fine 33, and young Jimmy Jameson chipped in with an unbeaten 24, but in between there was a collapse of the sort that makes a captain reach for something stronger than tea: 56 for 3 became 67 for 6 almost before I'd finished my sandwich, with Herbert Rayner and D Paine both departing for golden ducks.

As for the skipper, the less said the better — a first-ball blob, out without troubling the scorers. They do say a captain should lead by example; I led by example of exactly how not to bat.

With the ball we set about defending our total, and the innings simply ran out of overs before it ran out of wickets or patience — honours shared, a draw, neither side able to force the issue.

There was, at least, one thing to raise a glass to: Jimmy's 24 runs eased him past F E Wallis to 20th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, a fine achievement on a day short of other cheer. Crawford, meanwhile, sits proudly atop our 1945 run charts. A curious afternoon, then — honours even, pride dented, but the old averages ticking quietly onward.

Sunday 6th May, 1945, and off we trundled with the bat still smelling faintly of linseed and hope. Gordon Fenner was magnificent, 3 for 20 with two maidens thrown in for good measure, and had the very fine support of Stan Wratten (2-14) and Herbert Rayner (2-18) — not a run given away lightly, as the saying goes.

And then, dear reader, we batted. E-Humphreys fought the good fight for 32, but the rest of us contrived to leave the innings all out for what the book rather bluntly records as "None" — a scoreline that speaks for itself, and not kindly.

Still, silver linings: Gordon Fenner brought up his 100th career wicket, a landmark not to be sniffed at, while Humphreys's 32 nudged him past J N Tonge to 12th on the club's all-time run charts, and Wratten's brace lifted him past T Gandy into 23rd among the wicket-takers.
