Sun 01 Oct 1989 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformDDDLL

It was a Sunday to bat first and no argument about it — win the toss, have a hit, was very much the order of the day. On paper we did the job: 173 for 6 off our 25 overs, built on a lovely stand of 58 for the third wicket between Tim Thirkell (48) and Nigel Thirkell (37), with Bob Lines steady and unbeaten on 27 to see the innings home. I even chipped in with 37 myself, which felt rather grand at the time.

Not everything went to plan, mind. Poor Allan Cooper marched out, marched back, and left with a duck to his name — these things happen to the best of us, and he took the ribbing with good grace. Credit too to the extras column, which weighed in with a hearty 30 — some would say our most consistent performer of the summer.

Elsewhere in the runs charts, Nigel continues to sit proudly second in the 1989 1st XI standings on 880, Tim leads the Married table with 48, and David Castle tops the Singles with 58 — proof, if nothing else, that somebody in this club knows what they're doing with a bat.

As for the match itself: 173 ought to have been defendable on any decent Sunday, but the visitors had other ideas, cruising home by 8 wickets. Not our day, then. Heads up, chins forward, and back to the nets we go.

Sat 30 Sep 1989 🧢 1st XIv HC Hastings & St Leonards Priory CC · HomeformDWLDL

Saturday 30th September, and I called it right for once — coin came down my way, and with a hint of dampness in the air I fancied our seam bowlers before I fancied our batting, so in we put Hastings & St Leonards Priory.

It looked the correct call for a good while. Jon Harris steamed in for 4-40, with Nigel Thirkell doing the mean, miserly work at the other end — 4-48 and four maidens, not a run given away without a fight. But B. Phillips had other ideas, compiling a proper fifty (51), well supported by Tim Booth-Jones (36) and I. Phillips (25), and a stubborn 53-run sixth-wicket stand dragged the visitors to a competitive 184/8 from their 49.1 overs.

Chasing it down, we never quite got away, closing on 175/7 — honours even, in truth. But what a way to fall two runs short of the job: Nigel Thirkell, having bowled his heart out in the morning, then batted through the innings for a superb 95, the bulk of our total built on his shoulders alone. I'll confess a personal note of satisfaction too — 95 with the bat myself, and four wickets to boot, the kind of afternoon a captain doesn't get too often and certainly won't complain about. Amid it all, Tim Thirkell quietly brought up 7,000 career runs for the club — a landmark rather lost in the shuffle, but not by us.

A draw, then, and a fair one — neither side could quite land the killer blow. Two fine Thirkell efforts, honours shared, and everyone home in time for tea.

🗓️ 23 Sep – 29 Sep 1989 · 1989 — 2 matches

Wed 27 Sep 1989 🧢 Midweek XIv Penshurst Park CC · AwayformDDWLW

Under leaden Wednesday skies we rolled into Penshurst Park, and rather against habit the coin actually did as it was told, so I had no hesitation in putting us in.

The lads batted with real purpose. Andrew Hales set the tone with a well-crafted 53, and once he and Piers McDonald (48) had put on a crucial 97 for the second wicket, the innings had a spring in its step that even our tea urn couldn't dampen. Nigel Thirkell chipped in with 43, and 219 for 5 off our 42 overs felt like a total with a bit of menace to it.

Penshurst, though, had other ideas, chiefly in the form of I Mitchell, who ground out a fine 95 and, ably supported by M Latter's tidy fifty, dragged them into contention with an 86-run sixth-wicket stand. For a while we feared the worst. But cricket has a way of rewarding patience, and under pressure they subsided from 204 for 6 to 211 for 9 — three ducks (commiserations to P Grayland, John Hyde and F Manville) among the wreckage. Alan Boyns was the chief architect, 3 for 37 with four miserly maidens, well backed by Martin Larkin and Nigel Thirkell, both 2 for 19.

In the end neither side could quite land the killer blow, and honours were shared. A draw, then — honest, hard-fought, and entirely in keeping with the spirit of the game. We'll take it, and head home already looking forward to the next one.

Sun 24 Sep 1989 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformWDWLD

Beaten but not bowed, then — 24th September and Anchorians came calling. Winning the toss, I had no hesitation in taking first knock, and for once the decision looked shrewd rather than rash. Tappin's 47 and a stubborn 53-run third-wicket stand kept them in the hunt, and fifteen byes past poor Peter Whyte's gloves proved a costly afternoon's leakage. They crept home by the narrowest of margins — one wicket.

A word of real cheer amid the gloom: Jon Harris brought up 1600 career wickets, a landmark worth toasting in the bar regardless of the result. David Castle continues to top our 1989 run charts, Harris sits second among the wicket-takers. As for the match itself — cruelly close, honestly earned, and we'll be considerably better for it next week.

Sun 17 Sep 1989 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformWWDWL

Sunday last, 17 September, we welcomed Dummy Site to Linton Park, and for once the coin behaved itself — heads, and into bat we went. What followed was rather more than I dared hope for: 208 for 3 off 42.3 overs, built on a second-wicket stand of 162 that did most of the heavy lifting. Trevor Back played beautifully for his 78, and I'll confess to rather enjoying my own afternoon in the middle, reaching three figures — a hundred that, I'm reliably informed, also brought up 21,000 career runs for the club. Not bad for an old pair of legs that complain rather more than they used to.

In the field we chipped away without ever quite finishing the job. Dave Todd was miserly and menacing in equal measure — 2 for 35 with five maidens, the sort of spell that has batsmen playing for their suppers — while Stewart Morrison (2-50) and Ian Brattle (1-15) chipped in. W Creed made a determined 67 and P Dann held firm on 20 not out to see them home to a respectable 143 for 7 off their 46, safely short of us but safely out of our reach too.

So honours shared, a draw when neither side could quite force the issue. With David Castle and Matt Large still setting the pace atop this season's charts, there's plenty to play for yet — but a century and a personal milestone in the same afternoon is not a bad way to spend a Sunday.

We beat The Mote CC by 5 wickets in a run chase on a September Wednesday.

Mike Cutler was the chief architect with a well-crafted 44, with Dave McInnes (33) and Nigel Mullarkey (33 not out) lending sturdy support, the pair putting on 55 for the 7th wicket just when we thought we had them contained. 185 is never a formality on a Mote pitch, but Paul Gibson had other ideas. He and David Castle put on 79 for the second wicket to set the platform, then Gibson simply refused to get out, finishing unbeaten on a magnificent 85. Castle's 51 and Trevor Back's brisk 41 filled in the rest of the picture as we cantered home on 186/2 with overs to spare — five wickets in hand and, frankly, more comfort than the scoreline suggests. A tidy footnote for the scorebook: Gibson's innings nudges him up to 14th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, past Malcolm Pay, while Castle continues to sit 3rd in this season's Midweek run charts and Nigel Morton 2nd among the wicket-takers.

All told, a thoroughly good day out — professional in the field, ruthless with the bat, and home in time for tea.

My poor wicketkeeper had a torrid time of it too, with 15 byes leaking through — not his afternoon, bless him.

Beaten by five wickets in the end. Thirkell's purple patch continues, and Boyns leads our wicket charts — silver linings enough to send us home unbowed. We'll be better for it next week.
