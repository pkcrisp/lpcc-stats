Sun 30 Sep 1956 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLDDDL

Sunday 30th September, and by rights we should still be out there padded up somewhere, given how little cricket we actually witnessed. We hosted Linton Park CC, I won the toss, and — in a decision I shall be defending at the bar for some weeks yet — elected to bat first.

It did not go well. Forty-two all out in 34.1 overs is the sort of total that makes the tea urn seem the highlight of the afternoon. Basil Sears, Peter Weedon and Monty Deakes all contrived to depart without troubling the scorer, a hat-trick of ducks that had the wives in the pavilion reaching for their knitting rather than their scorecards. Quite how three good men can queue up to be bowled for nought in the same innings I shall leave to greater cricketing minds than mine.

And yet — cricket, glorious and infuriating, found a way to spare our blushes. Neither side could force home the advantage, and honours were shared in a draw that flattered us rather more than our batting deserved.

Elsewhere, the season's ledger continues to make cheerful reading regardless of Sunday's efforts: John Thirkell sits proudly atop the 1st XI run charts with 683 runs, John Taylor leads the Married men with 7, and Clive Smith heads the Singles with 33.

Verdict: a match we were fortunate to escape with a draw, and one best filed under "character-building."

Sun 23 Sep 1956 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDDWWW

Sunday 23rd September saw us at home, and having called correctly for once — a rare enough event to note in the log — I had no hesitation in batting first. It proved a sound decision. Ray Howard set the tone with a splendid 61, and William Buck, unbeaten on 51, played the innings of a man entirely unbothered by the occasion. Between them we posted 158 for 5 from our 47.4 overs, a total that felt, even as we walked off, like it might just be enough.

John Ames was magnificent, five for 21 with four maidens thrown in for good measure, bowling with the sort of miserly control that leaves batsmen muttering to themselves. I chipped in with 5 for 32 myself, which I shall be mentioning at the bar for weeks to come. All out for 78 in 30.4 overs — a fine afternoon's work.

Stan Wratten continues to sit proudly third in the season's run charts, and John Ames now leads the wickets with 68 — form he showed no sign of relinquishing today. A thoroughly satisfying win, and one to bottle for the wetter Sundays ahead.

Sat 22 Sep 1956 🧢 1st XIv Bearsted CC · HomeformLDDWW

Wratten's 29 propping up a total everyone else seemed determined to give away, five wickets from Ray Howard turning defence into ambush, and thirteen byes that had poor Bob Arnold's afternoon written all over it — a proper vintage Linton Park scorecard.

Skipper's Log, Saturday 22nd September 1956. Bearsted CC, at home, and for once the coin behaved itself — I called it right and, with barely a flicker of doubt, sent us in. Whether that flicker should have lasted rather longer is a question I've been turning over ever since, because ninety-seven all out from 38.1 overs is not the sort of total you build a Saturday around. Stan Wratten did what he could with a defiant 29, but around him the innings rather fell away, Bob Arnold and C Sandford both departing for ducks and leaving the rest of us to make up the numbers as best we might.

Ninety-seven felt, frankly, like a target invented purely to be exceeded, and I confess I walked out to field with the resigned air of a man expecting the worst. Instead Ray Howard decided the afternoon belonged to him, tearing through Bearsted for 5-15 with six miserly maidens thrown in for good measure, well supported by John Ames's 4-21 and a single strike from Tony Snell. F Bayley and Peter Longley both fell for ducks of their own, and Bearsted were bowled out for 56 in 32.1 overs. Thirteen byes did rather undermine Bob Arnold's afternoon behind the stumps, though nobody was counting too closely by the close.

We got over the line by 41 runs — a scoreline that flattered the batting and did full justice to the bowling. A fine, if faintly undeserved, day for the club.

🗓️ 15 Sep – 21 Sep 1956 · 1956 — 2 matches

Sun 16 Sep 1956 🧢 1st XIv TC Tunbridge Wells CC · HomeformWLDDW

Tuesday, and I'll admit I slept rather well after Sunday's exertions. Winning the toss and choosing to bat felt like the obvious call, though I confess I say that every week whether we bat first or not — this time, mercifully, the pitch and Tom Osborn agreed with me. What followed was as fine an opening stand as this club has produced in some while: 176 for the first wicket, an innings of such serenity that several of us on the boundary had time to finish our tea before a wicket fell. Then, as cricket so loves to remind us, the game turned briefly comic — 176 for one became 187 for four in what felt like the time it takes to pour another cup, poor Ray Howard departing without troubling the scorers. Osborn, though, was unmoved by the chaos around him, sailing serenely to 103 not out, well supported by Stan Wratten's 69. We closed on 195 for 4.

If our batting had its wobble, our bowling had none. Tunbridge Wells were bowled out for a meagre 76, undone almost entirely by John Ames, who span a web few of them escaped — 9 for 33, six of those overs maidens, figures that verge on the unsporting. Tony Snell chipped in for good measure, and three of their number trooped back without scoring a run between them.

A word for the history books: Osborn's century lifts him to 11th on the club's all-time run list, past Maurice Fenner, while Wratten and Ames continue to lead our season charts with real distinction.

A fine win by 119 runs, then, and thoroughly deserved. Onwards.

A draw, then — honours shared, and not an unjust one. We were outbatted and out-bowled them in equal measure, which is about as fair a way as cricket has of calling it even.
