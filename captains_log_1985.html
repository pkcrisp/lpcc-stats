🗓️ 28 Sep – 04 Oct 1985 · 1985 — 1 match

Sat 28 Sep 1985 🧢 1st XIv HC Hastings & St Leonards Priory CC · HomeformWDWDL

Saturday last, we welcomed Hastings & St Leonards Priory CC to Linton Park, and having won the toss I fancied a bat first without a moment's hesitation — a decision I stood by for the rest of the afternoon, whatever the state of my knees suggested by close of play. We were bowled out for 152 for 8 off our 48, Paul Bowles top-scoring with a fine 32, Nigel Thirkell chipping in 25 and Sean Charlton 23. I managed a workmanlike 22 myself, which in this company counts as a triumph.

Defending 152 always felt like a job for nerve rather than muscle, and so it proved. Alan Booth-Jones threatened to run away with it on 27, well supported by S Leach (23) and an unbeaten 22 from Ian Gillespie, but three of their number — Paul Grace, Geoff Collins and D Rafferty — went back to the pavilion without so much as a run to their name. Paul Bowles was the chief architect of that discomfort, a superb 4 for 37 including five miserly maidens, with Jon Harris (3-30) and Matt Large (1-21) offering fine support from the other end. Poor David Castle had a torrid time behind the stumps, though — eight byes is not a figure he'll want dwelling on in the tea hut.

In the end honours were shared, a draw when neither side could quite land the killer blow. With Bowles bowling like that and Thirkell still topping the season's run charts, I left the ground more encouraged than disappointed.

Wed 25 Sep 1985 🧢 Midweek XIv Penshurst Park CC · AwayformWDLLL

Wednesday brought us to Penshurst Park, and winning the toss I elected to bat — a decision I shall take full credit for, at least until someone reminds me otherwise. It wasn't the tidiest of starts, a wicket down with the board still reading nought, and there were moments when it threatened to unravel further, with both David Castle and Nigel Thirkell back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers. But cricket has a way of rewarding patience, and Andrew Hales played the innings of the day with a fine 49, Paul Bowles anchoring things beautifully with an unbeaten 43, and Jim Hall chipping in with 22. A stand of 64 for the seventh wicket did the heavy lifting, and then, in true tail-end fashion, our last pair conjured a defiant 58 to haul us to a respectable 166 for 9 from our 40 overs.

With the ball, it was Nigel Thirkell's afternoon to make amends in style, running through Penshurst's order for a magnificent 5 for 25 — proof, if any were needed, that a duck with the bat is no predictor of form with the ball. Paul Bowles backed him up with 3 for 45 and Jon Harris took 1 for 43, while N Fowler, P Johnson and Michael Meade all departed for ducks of their own. Mark Jenner's 40, with support from P Grayland's 34 and C Fry's unbeaten 25, threatened to spoil our afternoon, but Penshurst fell 11 runs short, all out for 155 from 38 overs.

A thoroughly good day for the club, and a result the lads deserved.

Sun 22 Sep 1985 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformWWDWD

Sunday 22nd September, and Anchorians came calling on a day that promised much and, in the end, delivered rather more drama than my nerves could comfortably take. Beaten by two wickets, then — the sort of margin that has you replaying every over on the walk home. Thirkell continues to lead our run charts and Harris our wicket charts this season, so the class is evidently there. We'll be better for it next week.

Sat 21 Sep 1985 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · AwayformLWWDW

From the captain's chair — Saturday 21 September 1985. We travelled to West Farleigh CC with the sun cooperating even if the toss did not, and I lost the coin flip for the umpteenth time this season, condemning us to field first.

They made hay to the tune of 172/7 from their 47 overs, Micky Back top-scoring with a fluent 45, Trevor Back weighing in with 30 and Tim Baker 23. It could have been considerably worse but for Nigel Thirkell, tight as a drum with 2-19 and three maidens to boot, while Paul Bowles and Matt Large chipped away either side of him for two apiece. A word too for Paul, who during all this quietly brought up 1000 career wickets — the kind of landmark that deserves a pint bought in his honour, and I daresay several were.

Chasing 172, we wobbled alarmingly to 6-1 before David Castle and Nigel Thirkell steadied the ship with a fine 90-run stand. Castle made a handsome 55, and Thirkell simply carried on where he left off with the ball, finishing unbeaten on 67 — a captain's innings if ever there was one. Matt Large added a brisk 20, and poor Malcolm Pay collected a golden duck for his troubles, bless him.

We finished level at 172/8, honours shared and nobody quite able to land the killer blow. A draw, then — fair reflection of an afternoon where both sides gave as good as they got, and Thirkell's all-round heroics kept our unbeaten skipper's blushes at bay.

Sat 14 Sep 1985 🧢 1st XIv SC Southborough CC · HomeformDLLWW

Saturday 14 September 1985 — Southborough CC came calling, the coin fell their way, and yours truly trudged back to the pavilion having lost the toss yet again. Small mercies: we got first use of the ball, which on a day like this counted for something.

Southborough limped to just 5 for the loss of their first wicket and we allowed ourselves a quiet smile, but Adam Hookway (36) and David Thorpe (31) steadied things, and an unbroken 52 for the eighth wicket between Barry Hart (31 not out) and company dragged them to a respectable 159 for 8 off their 46. The story of the innings, though, was Matt Large, who bowled with the discipline of a man who'd skipped his tea to concentrate — 5 for 53 with four maidens, a five-for thoroughly earned. Sean Charlton and Jon Harris chipped in with a wicket apiece for good measure.

In reply we were, by our own modest standards, positively serene about it all — 57 for 2 off 17 overs, Nigel Thirkell unbeaten on 24 and looking entirely unbothered by the whole affair, which is rather becoming his trademark; he now leads our 1985 run charts with 1,489 to his name, and Jon Harris tops the wicket pile with 75.

So, honours shared, a draw when neither side had the stomach or the overs to force it. Not a classic for the ages, but a thoroughly pleasant Saturday's cricket — and with bowling like Large's on show, I'll happily lose a few more tosses if it means watching that again.

Twenty runs the margin in the end — respectable in defeat, but defeat nonetheless. We'll dust ourselves down and come again next week.
