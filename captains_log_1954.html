Sun 26 Sep 1954 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLDD

As the honorary match reporter, I don't need tools for this — just the facts supplied. Here's the report:

Sunday last, and the fixture list was kind enough to bring the visitors to Linton Park, where I won the toss and, in a fit of optimism I can only put down to the weather, elected to bat. What followed was a tale of two very different tempos: 12 for 1 off our first four overs suggested a side in no particular hurry, but the innings settled beautifully thereafter, built almost entirely around one magnificent effort at the top of the order.

John Ames was simply immense, carrying his bat to 95 not out in an innings of real substance — the sort that has the tea urn conversations running long after the close. He had capable company too, with J. Coomber's 38 and Tony Tipples's 23 giving the innings proper backbone. I'll confess to a quiet satisfaction of my own, leading from the front with 95 of my own runs, though on a day like this it felt entirely secondary to the main event.

For it was rather a landmark afternoon for John: 1,000 career runs for the club now safely banked, and his 95 sees him leapfrog Richard Harris into 14th on our all-time run-scorers' list — a fine piece of arithmetic for a Sunday. He sits proudly atop the 1954 Married run charts, while young Stan Wratten leads the Singles.

In the end neither side could force the issue, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but one that will live long in the memory for reasons rather more personal than the scoreboard suggests.

Sat 25 Sep 1954 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · AwayformWDDDW

The road to West Farleigh CC does not go quickly at the best of times, and Saturday brought the added indignity of losing the toss — though watching what unfolded, I'm not sure the coin did us any disservice. Put into the field, we found our range early: John Thirkell, relentless as ever, took 4-24, while John Taylor span a web around the home side, 6-64 with six maidens thrown in for good measure, unplayable by any reasonable definition of the word. Reg Fermor, A Heard and R Gibbons all fell without troubling the scorers, and only S Kitchen's 27 kept West Farleigh from complete embarrassment as they folded for 114 in 36.4 overs.

Chasing 115, we made harder work of it than the bowling perhaps deserved, but Ron Aiston batted with real class for an unbeaten 78, well supported by John Basten's 21 and a tidy 13 from Tom Osborn, seeing us home at 168 for 5 with overs to spare. I even found some rhythm myself with the ball, four wickets to show for it.

Milestones worth raising a glass to: John Thirkell passed a hundred career wickets and climbed past Stan Wratten into 8th on the all-time list, while Tom Osborn's runs nudged him past John Ames into 15th. Tony Levick, meanwhile, sits second in this season's run charts with 347.

A comfortable five-wicket win, and a thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work. Onwards.

🗓️ 18 Sep – 24 Sep 1954 · 1954 — 2 matches

Sun 19 Sep 1954 🧢 1st XIv TC Tunbridge Wells CC · HomeformDWDDD

It was a canny toss to call and I'll say so myself: on winning it, elect to field was the only decision worth making on Sunday 19th September, and Tunbridge Wells CC duly obliged us with a target to chase rather than a mountain to defend.

They battled to 149/9 off their 51.4 overs, with Charles Wicker constructing a handsome fifty and Alan Burton and James Scrace lending sturdy support either side of a third-wicket stand of 57 that threatened, briefly, to get away from us. But get away it did not — we tightened the screws from 134-5 to 146-8 in the blink of an eye, poor Reg Hobbins departing without troubling the scorers. The architect of the collapse was Gordon Fenner, spinning a web for 6-45 and stingy enough with it to send down three maidens, while Ray Tippen chipped in with 2-24 and, almost incidentally, his fiftieth career wicket.

Chasing 150, we made handsomely light work of it, cruising home on 151/2 from 33 overs. Tony Levick anchored things beautifully with 45 not out, ably assisted by William Buck's 31 and John Thirkell's unbeaten 26 — with the extras column contributing a generous 32 of its own. I even found time to squeeze out 26 runs myself, more by accident than design.

A day for the history books besides: Stan Wratten brought up his hundredth Linton appearance, Levick and Tippen both climbed the all-time club charts, and we got over the line by eight wickets. All told, a thoroughly good day for the club.

Sat 18 Sep 1954 🧢 1st XIv Bearsted CC · HomeformLDWDD

Saturday saw us welcome Bearsted CC, and having called correctly at the toss, I didn't spend more than half a second thinking about it before opting to bat — some decisions make themselves. What followed was a proper treat: 188 for the loss of just one wicket in 44 overs, built on a marvellous unbroken innings of 92 from Tom Osborn and fine support from Basil Sears, who cruised nicely past his own fifty to finish on 89. Two lads well set and, frankly, making the rest of us look rather superfluous with the bat.

Bearsted's reply never quite got away from us, though J Bastin batted with real composure to see them to 78 for 4 from his 35 overs, unbeaten on 45 and doing most of the carrying himself. Our bowling was the story of the innings — Michael Morton probing away for figures of 2 for 19, with Stan Wratten and Ray Tippen chipping in a wicket apiece. Special mention for Ray, who bowled six maidens and simply refused to be hit off his length; not a run given away lightly all afternoon.

With Morton now sitting second in the club's 1954 wicket-takers list with 38 to his name, the season's shaping up nicely for him. In the end neither side could force it, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but on this evidence, a thoroughly satisfying one to be part of.

🗓️ 04 Sep – 10 Sep 1954 · 1954 — 1 match

Sun 05 Sep 1954 🧢 1st XIv GK Gillingham CC, Kent · HomeformLLLLD

As Skipper of Linton Park, on Sunday 5th September 1954, we welcomed Gillingham CC of Kent to our ground, and I lost no time at the toss in choosing to bat first — a decision I've thought about a good deal since, given what followed.

We laboured somewhat to 172/8 from our 47 overs, saved from mediocrity by Tony Levick, whose 83 was the innings of the day, well supported by William Buck's tidy 43. Elsewhere the top order rather fell on its sword — John Larking, John Thirkell and Richard Harris all departed for ducks, and I must confess to adding my own name to that unhappy list. The less said the better, as I told the wife.

Defending 172 looked a tall order until Gordon Fenner got his hand on the ball and produced a spell of real quality — five for thirty-nine, with four maidens to boot, bowling as tightly as a Sunday sermon. John Taylor chipped in with three for thirty and Alan Wilkes picked up one, and between them Gillingham were bundled out for 134 in 35.1 overs, A Fairbrass's fifty-seven the only real resistance. Eleven byes did creep past poor Tony Levick behind the stumps, but nobody was counting too closely by the close.

Amid the drama, Tony Levick brought up 1,000 career runs for the club, and William Buck's innings lifted him to the top of our all-time run-scorers. A fine, hard-fought 38-run victory, and two points thoroughly banked.
