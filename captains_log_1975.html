Sun 21 Sep 1975 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformWLWDW

I'll won by 4 runs, 93 all out then bowling them out for 89 — got it.

Sunday 21 September 1975, and Anchorians CC came to Linton Park for what turned out to be one of those afternoons that reminds you why you bother with this ridiculous game. I won the toss and had no hesitation in batting first — the right call, I still maintain, whatever the scorebook might suggest about our efforts once we got out there. Ninety-three all out in 37.5 overs was hardly a declaration of intent, and the Duck Club had a busy afternoon of it, with John Thirkell, Jon Harris and Tony Gauna all making that lonely walk back for nought. I can't claim the moral high ground either, having contributed nothing myself — we shall draw a veil over that, as I always do when it's my own name in the ledger.

Somehow, gloriously, it was enough. Anchorians replied with 89 all out in 38.2 overs, undone chiefly by Nigel Thirkell, who ran through them with 5-25 and six miserly maidens besides — the sort of spell that makes a captain's job absurdly easy. Ron Hickman chipped in with 4-20 and Tony Gauna, redeeming his duck rather handsomely, took 1-17. L. Neate went for nought at the other end, which felt only fair.

Four runs. Two points. Thoroughly undeserved on our batting, thoroughly earned on our bowling — and with Nigel Thirkell now sitting atop both the season's run and wicket charts, one wonders what the man can't do. A scrappy, nervy, entirely typical win. We'll take it.

Sat 20 Sep 1975 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · HomeformLWLWD

As it stands, the toss went our way and I had no hesitation putting West Farleigh in the field. 134/6 off 43 overs wasn't quite fireworks, but Nigel Thirkell's unbeaten 47 gave it real backbone, with Paul Bowles (39) and Paul Brattle (22) chipping in nicely. Poor Jim Hall, meanwhile, made the long walk back for nought — the sort of duck that gets you a mention in the ledger whether you like it or not.

Nigel continues to lead both the run charts and the wicket charts for the season, and on this evidence it's not hard to see why. A fine win by 46 runs — onwards we go.

🗓️ 13 Sep – 19 Sep 1975 · 1975 — 1 match

Sat 13 Sep 1975 🧢 1st XIv SC Southborough CC · HomeformWLWLW

13 September 1975 — Southborough CC at home.

Won the toss, and with a rare flash of tactical clarity I put us in — a decision that looked considerably better once Paul Bowles got going than it had any right to. He played beautifully for his 81, the kind of innings that makes the teas taste sweeter, and found a willing ally in John Ames, whose watchful 20 shored things up nicely. The middle order, alas, rather fell through a trapdoor — both John and Tim Thirkell contributed golden ducks apiece, proof that the Thirkell name alone cannot guarantee runs, only enthusiasm. We closed on 168/8 from our 44 overs, a total that felt like it had been carried home on one man's shoulders.

As for the captain — well, the less said the better. A golden blob, out without troubling the scorers, which rather undercuts any pre-match talk I'd given about leading from the front. Leading by example is all very well until the example is a blob.

In the field we held our nerve if not much else, restricting Southborough to 11/0 from their 7 overs before bad light or good sense brought us together on a draw — honours shared, and probably fair enough.

The day belonged, in truth, to Alan Cradduck, who brought up his 100th appearance in Linton colours — a wonderful milestone, and a reminder of what this club is really about. Meanwhile Nigel Thirkell continues to top the season's run charts with 619 to his name, quietly getting on with the job the rest of us keep threatening to do.

A draw, then — no losers, but with that top order, no complaints either.

Sun 31 Aug 1975 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformWDDWL

As the very last summer of a decade whose cricket we'll all remember for the heat rolls on, we trundled down to Staplehurst on a fine Sunday, lost the toss, and were duly asked to field — never my favourite way to start a Sunday, but there we are.

It turned out rather well for us. Paul Bowles bowled like a man possessed, spinning through their order for 5-18 with three tidy maidens thrown in for good measure, and Staplehurst never really recovered, folding for 149 despite useful knocks from B Thurlow (31), C Rogers (29) and Nigel Bardsley (23). Nigel Thirkell and Brian Piper chipped in with two apiece, and we even managed three ducks along the way — M Pontin, Bev Taylor and G Chipperfield all back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers. Jim Hall had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, mind you, with 20 byes going begging — I shall have a quiet word about that over tea.

With 150 to chase, Paul Bowles simply carried on where he'd left off, striding to 97 not out and dragging us home almost single-handed, well supported by Robert Chance (30) and Paul Brattle (20), as we cantered to 153/3. Not a bad day at the office for the skipper either, it must be said — five wickets with the ball to go with the runs.

Two points, thoroughly earned, seven wickets to spare. If Bowlesy keeps this up we may have to rename the club after him.

David Tolhurst fought a lone and rather excellent hand of 71 for the visitors, well supported by Mick Brown's 21, but three more Dummy Site batsmen went for ducks and the innings folded at 130. Eight runs the margin, two points banked, and Nigel's nine wickets nudge him past Tony Snell into 11th on the club's all-time list. A thoroughly deserved win, snatched from what looked, at tea, like a moderate total.
