Skipper's Log — Sunday 17 September 1944

Well, we did lose, but not before giving the good people of Linton Park something to natter about over their teas. Put in to field first, we made early inroads count for little as the visitors settled — a first-wicket stand of 56 that had me rearranging the field more times than I care to admit. T Waller's 29, with Lewer and Patey chipping in 25 and 22 apiece, carried them to 132 for 5 before, mercifully, the floodgates opened at our end for a change — 135 all out, with X Wilson, K Faulkner and C Curtis sent packing without so much as a run to their name. Herbert Rayner was magnificent, 4 for 28 with a couple of maidens thrown in for good measure, and I managed to sneak in on the act myself with four wickets — old legs, it seems, still remember the way to the crease.

With the bat we made a nervous fist of it, 8 down for the loss of an early wicket before Stan Wratten (46) and John Anscomb (38) put on 88 together and dragged us to something respectable — 142 for 5, helped along by a generous 26 extras, for which we thank the opposition's accuracy, or lack thereof. Poor Maurice Fenner had a torrid time behind the stumps, nine byes going begging, and in the end four wickets did for us — beaten, but not disgraced.

Stan Wratten and Herbert Rayner continue to lead our charts with 336 runs and 18 wickets respectively this season, so the engine room is in good order even when the chassis rattles. We'll be better for it next week.

Saturday 16th September 1944, and a fine autumn afternoon at home for the club. I called correctly for once and, with barely a flicker of doubt, put us in to bat — a decision I shall claim full credit for regardless of what followed.

What followed was rather splendid. Stan Wratten and John Anscomb put together a second-wicket stand of 90 that had the tea urn nearly boiling over with excitement, Wratten finishing unbeaten on 64 and Anscomb falling agonisingly short of the same landmark on 56. We closed on 162/2 from our 45 overs — a total with enough runs in it to make the walk out to field feel almost cheerful.

The visitors made a proper fist of the chase. R Goodwin top-scored with 46, well supported by T Waller's 26 and X Rice's unbeaten 23, and for a while it looked as though our tea might go to waste. But Gordon Fenner bowled beautifully for 3-44 with two maidens to show for his miserliness, Stan Wratten chipped in with 2-3 (as if his 64 with the bat weren't enough for one afternoon), and D Cloke took 1-14. X Lever and C Makudo departed without troubling the scorers. They closed on 138/6.

A draw, then — honours shared, and honestly a fair reflection of an afternoon where neither side could quite land the killer blow. Stan Wratten's day, in truth: runs, wickets, and a climb past the Reverend himself into 8th on the all-time list. The rest of us merely watched.

Gordon Fenner was the story of the day — 5 for 30, a five-for that had been coming and was thoroughly earned, moving him past Ernest Cruttenden into 4th on the all-time wicket-takers list. A 61-run victory, two points banked, and Stan Wratten and Gordon Fenner both sitting proudly atop the 1944 season charts. All told, a very satisfactory afternoon's work.
