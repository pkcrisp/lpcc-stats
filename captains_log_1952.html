🗓️ 27 Sep – 03 Oct 1952 · 1952 — 1 match

Sun 28 Sep 1952 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformL

Sunday 28 September, and Linton Park travelled with hope in our hearts and, as ever, not quite enough jumpers between us for the weather. Winning the toss, I had no hesitation in sending us in to bat — a decision that looked rather better once Stan Wratten got going. Stan played an innings of real substance, 79 to his name, striking the ball with the sort of authority that makes the rest of us quietly reconsider our own techniques. John Ames matched him for class with a tidy 55, and Gordon Fenner chipped in with 20, as we motored to 35 for the loss of a single wicket in just six overs — brisk work by anyone's standards.

There was a personal landmark buried in all that scoring, too: Stan's 79 eased him past John Thirkell into second on the club's all-time run charts, a bit of history made between overs while most of us were still doing up our pads. He now sits proudly atop the 1952 Married run charts as well, while John Thirkell continues to lead the Singles table — the two of them waging their own quiet contest within the wider one.

In the end neither side could force a result, and honours were shared. A draw, then, but a thoroughly agreeable one — bowled along by a wonderful lesson in batting from Wratten and Ames, and quite possibly the most cheerful stalemate Linton Park has enjoyed all season.

🗓️ 20 Sep – 26 Sep 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Sun 21 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformLDLLL

Sunday, 21 September 1952, and the visitors came to Linton Park with runs in their boots. Put in first, we chipped away at them for the best part of fifty-two overs, Gordon Fenner leading the charge with a fine 4-34, well backed by Frank Taylor's tireless 2-46 — eight maidens from that man alone, miserly enough to make a Yorkshireman blush — and John Thirkell chipping in with 1-21. G Ashby's 43 was the innings that hurt us most, with H Gosling and T Ewell lending a hand, but we had them back in the hutch for 155, J Stinchcombe and C Pilbeam both departing without troubling the scorers.

Our reply was a more nervous affair. William Buck's 29 and Don Hartridge's 28 gave us something to build on, John Taylor chipping in a handy 20, but poor Richard Harris fell for a duck and we never quite found the fluency to chase the game down, settling for 131/7 from our thirty-three overs. Tony Levick had a torrid time behind the stumps, nine byes leaking through like water through a village-green drain.

Amid it all, a couple of milestones worth toasting in the clubhouse: Gordon Fenner's runs eased him past Tom Osborn into 17th on the all-time run charts, while Frank Taylor's two wickets vaulted him to the very top of the club's all-time wicket-takers. Honours shared, then — a draw, but one with rather more silverware in the small print than the scoreboard let on.

Sat 20 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDLDLL

We lost the toss on Saturday and were asked to make first use of the ball, which in Linton Park terms usually means twenty minutes of grumbling and then getting on with it. Dummy Site were shot out for 93 in 34 overs, tidy work all round, with John Taylor bowling like a man possessed — 6 for 32, five of those overs maidens, not a run given away lightly. Tony Snell chipped in with 3 for 35 and Ray Tippen picked up 1 for 18, his solitary wicket enough to nudge him past W H Fryer into 19th on the club's all-time list. Only Tiley, with a defiant 36, gave their innings any shape; the rest came and went, 24 for 2 collapsing to 30 for 5 before we knew where we were.

Chasing 94, we simply strolled it. Basil Sears played the innings of the day, 52 not out, calm as you like, and in doing so eased past Don Hartridge into 7th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — the sort of milestone you only notice once someone points it out. Stan Wratten offered fine support with 20 not out, keeping his season in rude health, second in the 1st XI charts with 671 runs, while Taylor's 58 wickets keep him out in front of the bowling table.

Beaten by nine wickets, and thoroughly deserved. Dummy Site simply had too little to bowl at. We'll dust ourselves down and come back stronger next week.

🗓️ 13 Sep – 19 Sep 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Sun 14 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDDLDL

Sunday 14 September saw Dummy Site roll into Linton Park with, it must be said, rather more spring in their step than we managed after losing the toss and being asked to field first — hardly ideal on a warm afternoon, but there we are.

The visitors made 122 all out from their 42 overs, G. Lukehurst the chief architect with a fine 62, while F. Dixon departed without so much as a nibble at the scoring. Honours, though, belonged to John Taylor, who span a web through their order for a magnificent 5-45 — utterly unplayable, and thoroughly deserved. John Ames (3-44) and Frank Taylor (2-7) tidied up the rest, with Tony Snell characteristically stingy, sending down four maidens and giving nothing away for love nor money.

Chasing 123, we got there in some comfort at 127/4 from 30.1 overs, John Thirkell the mainstay with an unbeaten 52, ably assisted by William Buck (32) and Stan Wratten (22) — their fourth-wicket stand of 67 the innings' backbone. I'll confess my own 52 gave me no small satisfaction.

Milestones abounded: Thirkell's knock lifts him to second on the club's all-time run charts, past Wratten himself, while Taylor's five-for edges him past Herbert Rayner into fourth among our wicket-takers. Both remain, unsurprisingly, streets ahead in this season's charts.

Beaten by six wickets, then — but with performances like these, we'll be better for it next week.

🗓️ 06 Sep – 12 Sep 1952 · 1952 — 1 match

Sat 06 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · AwayformLLDDL

Saturday 6 September 1952, away at Dummy Site, and for once the coin did what it was told. I put us in, and by the close I had very little to complain about.

John Thirkell simply took the day away from everyone — 120, a proper captain's-innings-stealer, and I should know, having contributed one myself of exactly the same number, which felt rather satisfying even if I say so. D Simmons chipped in a tidy 43 and Basil Sears 23, the two of them putting on 152 for the second wicket while I mostly stood at the other end admiring the view. 215/3 from 39 overs was a total with a bit of swagger to it.

In the field it was Frank Taylor's afternoon in every sense — 4/27, and in doing so he sailed past George Champion to sit top of the club's all-time wicket-takers, and popped his 200th career scalp along the way. Gerald Wright bowled beautifully in support, 3/20 with five maidens, mean as you like. Norris (44) and Stanley (37) threatened to drag them home, but between two ducks, a wobble at 2 for the loss of an early wicket, and our lot refusing to be shaken, they closed on 117/7. Eight byes did rather give the game away behind the stumps — a testing day for the gloves, we'll say no more.

A draw, then — honours shared, nobody quite able to land the killer blow. With Thirkell top of the season's run charts and Taylor now third in the wickets, I'd call that a thoroughly satisfactory way to spend a Saturday.

As for the skipper, the less said the better: a golden blob, proof that leading by example doesn't always extend to the scorebook. One shaft of light amid the gloom — Gordon Fenner passed 200 career wickets, a landmark worth raising a glass to.

In the end we fell 18 runs short. Not our finest hour, but not a disgrace either — we'll dust ourselves down and be better for it next week.

Sat 30 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv HC Hunton Wanderers CC · AwayformLLLLD

From the captain's chair — Saturday 30 August 1952, away at Hunton Wanderers CC, and the coin did us the courtesy of landing right side up. I had no hesitation in putting us in.

We reached 150/9 from our 47 overs, which felt rather more comfortable in the making than it sounds on paper. John Taylor led the way with a fine 54, well supported by D Simmons (26) and Arthur Cradduck (23), the pair sharing a sturdy 59 for the second wicket that gave the innings its spine. Then, as is our club's happy custom, the wheels came off with some enthusiasm — 98-2 became 108-5 before anyone had quite worked out what was happening, and poor John Thirkell and H Newport were both back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers.

In the field we found rather more discipline than we'd managed with the bat. Hunton replied with 66 for the first wicket, and A Pilcher made a classy 67 to anchor things, with G Lambell (29) and J Ludden (27) chipping in — but they too wobbled late, subsiding from 140-3 to 142-6, C Fenner departing for nought. John Thirkell was the pick of the attack with 3-27, and John Taylor, having done his bit with the bat, chipped in with 1-42 and three tidy maidens; Gerald Wright took 1-65. I contributed a duck with the bat, best drawn a veil over, but managed 3 wickets to salve my conscience.

A draw, then — honours shared, and honestly, after that batting collapse, we'll take it.

🗓️ 23 Aug – 29 Aug 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Sun 24 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDLLLL

It was a scorcher of a Sunday, 24 August 1952, and we welcomed the visitors home in fine spirits — though rather less fine cricket, if I'm honest.

Put into the field, the lads toiled manfully as the opposition ground us down to 235/6 from their 55 overs. We had them 7 for 1 and I dared allow myself a quiet smile, but Robin Bush (74 not out) and Jack Whibley (62) put paid to that with a stand of 63 for the third wicket, ably assisted by Don Capeling's 26. Their last pair then had the cheek to bolt on 45 runs we could frankly have done without — the sort of thing that makes a captain reach for the teapot rather early. John Ames led our attack heroically with 3-53, Stan Wratten chipped in with 2-55, and Tony Snell bowled a tidy 1-69 with two maidens to his name. Small mercy of the day: E Putwain, sent back without troubling the scorers.

With the bat we never quite got going, subsiding to 148/7 from our 47 overs. Tom Osborn held things together with a gritty 45, well supported by Stan Wratten (31) and Tony Levick (20), while I scratched about for 15 of my own. Ten byes rather gave the game away on Tom's afternoon behind the stumps. There was a silver lining, mind — that 45 nudged Tom past Gordon Fenner into 17th on the club's all-time run list, which called for at least one extra pint.

So honours shared, neither side able to force it home. A draw that flattered nobody, but on a day like this, I'll happily take it.

Sat 23 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv CA Charles Arkcoll · HomeformWDLLL

Here is the report.

Saturday last saw Charles Arkcoll roll into Linton Park, and having called it right at the toss, I had few doubts about batting first — a decision I still stand by, whatever the wobbles that followed. Losing our first wicket at 6 was not the beginning any captain dreams of, but the middle order stiffened admirably, and I was rather chuffed to lead from the front with 75 of my own, well supported by Arthur Cradduck's tidy 22 and a priceless sixth-wicket stand of 88. Alas, cricket has a way of humbling a man just as he's getting comfortable, and a collapse from 116-7 to 127 all out reminded us why the game is called a great leveller. Spare a thought for D Simmons, Gordon Bugge and Bob Arnold, who made the lonely walk for nought apiece — the Duck Club welcomes three new members.

In the field we scrapped hard without quite landing the killer blow. Their openers put on a breezy 77 before D Simmons prised things open with 2-23, backed nicely by P Ruddy and Bert Buckoke, while Gerald Wright bowled with the miserly discipline of a man guarding his allotment — seven maidens and barely a run given away. Randolph Thompson's 36, with Heathfield and Wratten in support, eventually saw them home by five wickets, our cause not helped by nine byes that poor Bob Arnold would rather forget.

A small mantelpiece consolation: my 75 nudges past Basil Sears into 7th on the all-time list. Otherwise, not our day — heads up, and back to the nets.

🗓️ 16 Aug – 22 Aug 1952 · 1952 — 1 match

Sun 17 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWDLL

Sunday 17 August 1952, and Dummy Site rolled up in fine weather to find the coin siding with us, which — reader, brace yourself — I promptly used to put us in to bat. Whether that was captaincy or self-sabotage remains under review. We limped to 132 all out, rescued in part by Gordon Fenner's unbeaten 45, and a heroic 9th-wicket stand of 54 that briefly suggested we knew what we were doing. Then, with the elegance of a deckchair collapsing, we slid from 63 for 5 to 68 for 8, Tony Snell and Gerald Wright both departing for ducks, presumably to go and stand somewhere quietly.

With ball in hand, though, we found our form. John Taylor was quite simply unplayable, spinning his way to 6 for 40 with 13 miserly maidens, backed up nicely by John Ames (2 for 17) and yours truly, who managed 2 for 30 and felt rather pleased about it. Dummy Site were bowled out for 108, Harman (27) and McCabe (21) doing most of the damage, while Waters and Stewart both bagged ducks of their own. William Buck had a torrid time behind the stumps, coughing up 18 byes, and I chipped in 19 with the bat for good measure.

Amid the wreckage, some cheer: Don Hartridge's 5 runs nudged him to 10th on the all-time run charts, and Gordon Fenner's 2 wickets lifted him to 2nd on the all-time wicket list. Beaten by 24 in the end — honest cricket, honestly lost. We'll be better for it next week.

Holloway (22) chipping in, and a stubborn 53-run stand for the seventh wicket did the real damage to our afternoon. Sixteen byes, though, tells you all you need to know about the state of things behind the stumps — a testing day for all concerned — and three more ducks, for Taylor, Dampier and Maynard, didn't help our cause.

🗓️ 02 Aug – 08 Aug 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Mon 04 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDDLWD

Lost the toss on Monday and put straight into the field — never my favourite way to spend an August afternoon, but there it is. Dummy Site set about us with real purpose, and one J- Pocock in particular decided he fancied a hundred, getting one too, unbeaten on 105 by the close of their innings. He had willing company from J- Larking (36) and Tom Osborn (26), and though we nabbed an early wicket at 2-1 to raise hopes of a tidy afternoon, the fourth-wicket pair put on 104 and rather took the game away from us. Credit where due: Gordon Fenner toiled magnificently for 2-52 with four maidens, John Ames chipping in with 1-35, but 206/4 from 48 overs was always going to be a stiff ask.

And so it proved. Maurice Fenner played beautifully for 52, Stan Wratten (33) and John Ames (24) lending fine support, and the openers gave us real hope with 70 for the first wicket. But wickets tumbled thereafter and we folded for 167, poor Gordon Fenner enduring the walk of shame for a duck to complete a rather mixed day with bat and ball.

Some sunshine amid the gloom: Stan Wratten brought up 2,000 runs for the club and still leads our 1952 run charts with 591, while Maurice Fenner's fifty eased him past Ernest Cruttenden into 14th on the all-time list — Gordon, meanwhile, sits third in the season's wicket-takers with 28. Milestones aplenty, but the scoreboard read defeat by 39 runs. Heads up, lads — back to the nets.
