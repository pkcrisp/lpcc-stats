🗓️ 27 Sep – 03 Oct 1952 · 1952 — 1 match

Sun 28 Sep 1952 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformL

Sunday 28 September, and Linton Park travelled with hope in our hearts and, as ever, not quite enough jumpers between us for the weather. Winning the toss, I had no hesitation in sending us in to bat — a decision that looked rather better once Stan Wratten got going. Stan played an innings of real substance, 79 to his name, striking the ball with the sort of authority that makes the rest of us quietly reconsider our own techniques. John Ames matched him for class with a tidy 55, and Gordon Fenner chipped in with 20, as we motored to 35 for the loss of a single wicket in just six overs — brisk work by anyone's standards.

There was a personal landmark buried in all that scoring, too: Stan's 79 eased him past John Thirkell into second on the club's all-time run charts, a bit of history made between overs while most of us were still doing up our pads. He now sits proudly atop the 1952 Married run charts as well, while John Thirkell continues to lead the Singles table — the two of them waging their own quiet contest within the wider one.

In the end neither side could force a result, and honours were shared. A draw, then, but a thoroughly agreeable one — bowled along by a wonderful lesson in batting from Wratten and Ames, and quite possibly the most cheerful stalemate Linton Park has enjoyed all season.

🗓️ 20 Sep – 26 Sep 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Sun 21 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformLDLLL

Sunday, 21 September 1952, and the visitors came to Linton Park with runs in their boots. Put in first, we chipped away at them for the best part of fifty-two overs, Gordon Fenner leading the charge with a fine 4-34, well backed by Frank Taylor's tireless 2-46 — eight maidens from that man alone, miserly enough to make a Yorkshireman blush — and John Thirkell chipping in with 1-21. G Ashby's 43 was the innings that hurt us most, with H Gosling and T Ewell lending a hand, but we had them back in the hutch for 155, J Stinchcombe and C Pilbeam both departing without troubling the scorers.

Our reply was a more nervous affair. William Buck's 29 and Don Hartridge's 28 gave us something to build on, John Taylor chipping in a handy 20, but poor Richard Harris fell for a duck and we never quite found the fluency to chase the game down, settling for 131/7 from our thirty-three overs. Tony Levick had a torrid time behind the stumps, nine byes leaking through like water through a village-green drain.

Amid it all, a couple of milestones worth toasting in the clubhouse: Gordon Fenner's runs eased him past Tom Osborn into 17th on the all-time run charts, while Frank Taylor's two wickets vaulted him to the very top of the club's all-time wicket-takers. Honours shared, then — a draw, but one with rather more silverware in the small print than the scoreboard let on.

Sat 20 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDLDLL

We lost the toss on Saturday and were asked to make first use of the ball, which in Linton Park terms usually means twenty minutes of grumbling and then getting on with it. Dummy Site were shot out for 93 in 34 overs, tidy work all round, with John Taylor bowling like a man possessed — 6 for 32, five of those overs maidens, not a run given away lightly. Tony Snell chipped in with 3 for 35 and Ray Tippen picked up 1 for 18, his solitary wicket enough to nudge him past W H Fryer into 19th on the club's all-time list. Chasing 94, we simply strolled it. Stan Wratten offered fine support with 20 not out, keeping his season in rude health, second in the 1st XI charts with 671 runs, while Taylor's 58 wickets keep him out in front of the bowling table.

Beaten by nine wickets, and thoroughly deserved. Dummy Site simply had too little to bowl at. We'll dust ourselves down and come back stronger next week.

🗓️ 13 Sep – 19 Sep 1952 · 1952 — 2 matches

Sun 14 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDDLDL

Sunday 14 September saw Dummy Site roll into Linton Park with, it must be said, rather more spring in their step than we managed after losing the toss and being asked to field first — hardly ideal on a warm afternoon, but there we are.

The visitors made 122 all out from their 42 overs, G. Lukehurst the chief architect with a fine 62, while F. Dixon departed without so much as a nibble at the scoring. Honours, though, belonged to John Taylor, who span a web through their order for a magnificent 5-45 — utterly unplayable, and thoroughly deserved. John Ames (3-44) and Frank Taylor (2-7) tidied up the rest, with Tony Snell characteristically stingy, sending down four maidens and giving nothing away for love nor money.

Chasing 123, we got there in some comfort at 127/4 from 30.1 overs, John Thirkell the mainstay with an unbeaten 52, ably assisted by William Buck (32) and Stan Wratten (22) — their fourth-wicket stand of 67 the innings' backbone. I'll confess my own 52 gave me no small satisfaction.

Milestones abounded: Thirkell's knock lifts him to second on the club's all-time run charts, past Wratten himself, while Taylor's five-for edges him past Herbert Rayner into fourth among our wicket-takers. Both remain, unsurprisingly, streets ahead in this season's charts.

Beaten by six wickets, then — but with performances like these, we'll be better for it next week.

Sat 06 Sep 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · AwayformLLDDL

Saturday 6 September 1952, away at Dummy Site, and for once the coin did what it was told. I put us in, and by the close I had very little to complain about.

Eight byes did rather give the game away behind the stumps — a testing day for the gloves, we'll say no more.

A draw, then — honours shared, nobody quite able to land the killer blow. With Thirkell top of the season's run charts and Taylor now third in the wickets, I'd call that a thoroughly satisfactory way to spend a Saturday.

Sat 30 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv HC Hunton Wanderers CC · AwayformLLLLD

From the captain's chair — Saturday 30 August 1952, away at Hunton Wanderers CC, and the coin did us the courtesy of landing right side up. I had no hesitation in putting us in.

Then, as is our club's happy custom, the wheels came off with some enthusiasm — 98-2 became 108-5 before anyone had quite worked out what was happening, and poor John Thirkell and H Newport were both back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers.

In the field we found rather more discipline than we'd managed with the bat. Hunton replied with 66 for the first wicket, and A Pilcher made a classy 67 to anchor things, with G Lambell (29) and J Ludden (27) chipping in — but they too wobbled late, subsiding from 140-3 to 142-6, C Fenner departing for nought. John Thirkell was the pick of the attack with 3-27, and John Taylor, having done his bit with the bat, chipped in with 1-42 and three tidy maidens; Gerald Wright took 1-65. I contributed a duck with the bat, best drawn a veil over, but managed 3 wickets to salve my conscience.

A draw, then — honours shared, and honestly, after that batting collapse, we'll take it.

Sat 23 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv CA Charles Arkcoll · HomeformWDLLL

Here is the report.

A small mantelpiece consolation: my 75 nudges past Basil Sears into 7th on the all-time list. Otherwise, not our day — heads up, and back to the nets.

Sun 17 Aug 1952 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWDLL

1952 was a golden vintage. Sunday, 17th August, with Dummy Site the visitors, the coin obliged and I stuck us in — a decision I spent the rest of the afternoon quietly regretting.

We limped to 132 all out from our 40 overs, and only just at that. Gordon Fenner batted with real sense for his unbeaten 45, and heaven knows we needed it, because the middle order took one look at the situation and fled: 63 for 5 became 68 for 8 before anyone could reach for the scorebook, with Tony Snell and Gerald Wright both departing for golden ducks. Beaten by 24 runs, then, but with milestones for Don Hartridge and Gordon Fenner to soften the blow. We'll be better for it next week.
