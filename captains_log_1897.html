Sat 21 Aug 1897 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDWLLD

Saturday the 21st of August, 1897, and we welcomed a touring side to Linton Park with the sun more or less behaving itself. I won the toss and had no hesitation in electing to bat — the right call, I still maintain, whatever anyone says over tea.

G Knight batted like a man possessed for a glorious 121, the sort of innings you tell your grandchildren about, while Jack Fryer (39 not out) and H G Smith (37) played the perfect supporting cast. The extras chipped in a generous 30 of their own, which I've always felt deserves its own place in the batting order. Not every man's luck held, mind — poor F Smith managed only a golden duck, and has my full sympathy over the sandwiches.

Then came the small matter of defending it. F Baker, J Bransome and E Taylor all departed without troubling the scorers, and Jack Fryer turned positively demonic with the ball, running through the order for nine wickets, well backed by H G Smith's one. I even managed to get through a spell myself, nine wickets to show for it, in between my thirty-nine runs — not a bad afternoon's work, even if I say so.

Special mention for G Knight, whose century now carries him past J N Tonge into 7th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — a name now etched rather higher up the honours board than mine.

In the end, though, we were beaten by an innings and 200 runs, which rather puts my captaincy decisions in perspective. Chins up, lads — we'll be better for it next week.

Wednesday the eleventh of August found us on the road to Town Malling, where I lost the toss for my sins and we were put out into the field. H G Smith backed him up handsomely with 4-0, and H Pile chipped in with one, while G Viner departed for a duck at our hands. Only H B Richardson, with a well-made 89, and useful contributions from W H Cooper (23) and J Todman (20), kept the innings respectable before it closed.

Our reply had its own quiet glories. George Champion, not content with his bowling exertions, top-scored with 45, F G Laurence made a tidy 44, and Jack Fryer weighed in with 39. I allowed myself a modest 45 of my own, and a happy afternoon with the ball too, taking five wickets for good measure — the sort of day a captain dares not admit he enjoyed quite so much.

There was history made besides: Champion's runs carried him past Arthur Cornwallis to the very top of the club's all-time run-scorers, while Smith's wickets took him past W Crittenden into sixteenth on the all-time list — and Champion, with 307 runs and 36 wickets this season, leads both first-team charts outright.

In the end neither side could force it, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but a thoroughly satisfying one to be part of.
