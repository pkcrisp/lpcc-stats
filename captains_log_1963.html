Sun 22 Sep 1963 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLLDDD

Sunday 22nd September saw us at home, and for once the coin obliged me — I called correctly and, with the sort of confidence that usually precedes disaster, stuck us in to bat. For a while it looked the right call. Dick Langridge played the innings of the day, a fine 48 that had the deckchairs murmuring approval, with Stan Wratten a steady 37 and Gordon Fenner a brisk 26 alongside him. We closed on 166 for 7 from our 42 overs, helped along by a generous 28 extras — the bowlers' unwitting contribution to the cause.

Not every man enjoyed his afternoon in the sun. Bob Arnold and Cliff Anscombe both endured that particular walk back to the pavilion reserved for the golden duck, heads high, pride rather more dented, and no doubt already plotting revenge with next week's bat.

The season's ledger continues to make interesting reading: Dick now leads the Married run charts on the strength of that 48, while Ray Howard sits handsomely atop the Singles table with 88 — a gap the rest of us married men would rather not discuss over tea.

As for the result, 166 was not, in the end, enough. We fell 28 runs short, beaten fair and square. Still, there was enough in the batting to suggest better days ahead, and if nothing else, the ducks gave the tea-hut something to talk about. We'll be better for it next week.

🗓️ 14 Sep – 20 Sep 1963 · 1963 — 2 matches

Sun 15 Sep 1963 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformLDWDW

From the captain's chair — Sunday 15 September 1963. We travelled to Staplehurst CC, and the coin, as it so often does when you need it most, let us down; they had first say and elected to bat.

Not that it did them much good in the end. Two down for one told its own story early on, and though there was a brief rally to 37 for 3, the floodgates then opened with alarming speed to 37 for 6 — Catt, Holden and Paine all departing without troubling the scorer. Only J- Blunt, unbeaten on 28, showed any real defiance. Michael Chambers was the chief architect of the collapse, spinning his way to 6 for 28 with four maidens thrown in for good measure — not a run given away lightly, that man. Alan Wilkes chipped in handsomely with 3 for 25, and David Gale, who on the day also brought up his hundredth career wicket, took 1 for 22. Staplehurst were bowled out for a modest 91 in 29.2 overs.

Chasing such a total ought to have been a formality, and while it never quite felt like one, we got there in the same 29.2 overs, finishing on 92 for 6. I confess to a certain pride in leading from the front with 54 not out, though I had precious little company — Norman Mattingley and Dick Langridge both fell for ducks. My glovework behind the stumps was less distinguished, 15 byes escaping my attention in what was, in fairness, a tricky afternoon.

A fine day all round, then, crowned by Michael Chambers's leap to 22nd on the club's all-time wicket-takers list. Four wickets, thoroughly earned, and won by four — I shan't grumble at the symmetry.

Sat 14 Sep 1963 🧢 1st XIv BC Bowaters CC · HomeformLLDWD

From the captain's chair — Saturday 14 September 1963. Bowaters CC were the visitors, and having lost the toss I did the only sensible thing a captain in my position can do, which is put a brave face on being asked to chase.

As it turned out, the field was rather the place to be. Bowaters never quite got their innings into second gear, wobbling to 8 for 1 early on and never truly recovering, before folding from 81 for 7 to 82 all out in the 36th over — William Preston's 36 the only real resistance. Alan Tucker span a web through them, 5 for 18, with H Mannering lending stout support at 4 for 21, and Terry Donovan tying an end down with a couple of maidens for good measure. Trevor Clinch and Colin Cole trudged back without opening their accounts, which I shall put down to the wicket rather than the will.

Eighty-three to win is never quite the formality the scorebook suggests, and so it proved when we lost an early wicket with just 3 on the board — the sort of nervy start that has spectators reaching for their flasks rather than their applause. But John Thirkell, who continues to lead our 1963 run charts and did nothing here to relinquish that crown, played the anchor role beautifully for 40, and I was glad to chip in with 40 of my own to see us home. Poor Roy Clarke had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, 10 byes testament to a pitch with ideas of its own.

Five wickets, two points, and thoroughly earned — a fine day's work all round.

🗓️ 07 Sep – 13 Sep 1963 · 1963 — 2 matches

Sun 08 Sep 1963 🧢 1st XIv HC Harrietsham CC · HomeformLLLDW

Sunday 8th September dawned fine over Linton Park, and with Harrietsham the visitors, winning the toss felt like the first stroke of luck we'd need all season. I had no hesitation in taking first knock — a decision I'll happily take credit for, since it's not often my hunches survive contact with reality.

John Ames set about proving me right with a fine 41, the kind of innings that makes a captain look wiser than he is, before Frank Brattle chipped in with an unbeaten 39 to help us to a tidy 103 for 1 off our 26 overs. One wicket down all afternoon rather rather undersold the control with which we batted — Harrietsham's bowlers must have wondered what they'd done to deserve such calm treatment. Brattle, for what it's worth, continues his quiet march up the season's run charts, now sitting second in the club's 1st XI standings with 396 to his name — a man clearly saving his best for when it matters least urgently, if such a thing exists in cricket.

Alas, for all our restraint with the bat, neither side could summon the wit or the weather to force a result, and honours were shared. A draw, then — the great English compromise, satisfying no one and offending no one. Still, with Ames purring and Brattle ominously in form, I left the field more encouraged than the scorebook alone might suggest. Onwards.

Saturday, 7th September 1963. Fine turnout to host St Lukes Maidstone, and the coin let us down before a ball was bowled — they won the toss and chose to bat, leaving us to make the early running with the leather.

Credit to the lads for steadying the ship — a stand of 50 for the second wicket set things right, D Simmons top-scoring with 42, Norman Mattingley (31) and John Thirkell (28) lending sturdy support, and I chipped in with 28 myself, for what it's worth. Frank Brattle and Terry Donovan both had quieter afternoons with the bat.

Three wickets to spare, 110-7 the final tally, and two points safely banked. A nervy watch from the boundary, but thoroughly earned all the same.

Saturday 24 August 1963, Aylesford Paper Mills at home, and I won the toss and fancied our chances with the bat. I fancied wrong.

Defending 58 is a job for miracle workers, and while we aren't quite that, we gave it a proper go. David Gale was the pick of us by some distance, mopping up 3 for 27 and, in the process, taking his season's tally to 43 wickets — a effort that deserves rather better support than we managed to give him. But Cliff Anscombe had other ideas, batting through unbeaten on 44 to see Aylesford home at 62 for 4 in just 12.2 overs.

Five wickets the margin, and a fair reflection of the day. Not our finest hour with bat or ball — heads up, chins slightly less so, and back to the nets we go.

Sat 17 Aug 1963 🧢 1st XIv BC Bowaters CC · AwayformLLLLD

1963, Bowaters CC — a Saturday to file quietly in the log and reach for the sherry rather than the champagne.

Winning the toss, I elected to bat, and I still maintain it was the right call — the pitch, the weather, the general vibe of the thing all pointed that way. What the pitch did not point towards was 7 for 1 inside the first exchanges, which rather set the tone for what followed. From there the innings did not so much collapse as gently disintegrate: 7 for 1 became 14 for 4 in the time it takes to pour a tea, and Roy Clarke, Dick Langridge and H Mannering each strode out, had a look around, and strode back again without troubling the scorer — three golden ducks in support of one another, which takes a certain camaraderie. We were all out for 55 in 22.4 overs, a total that flattered nobody.

But cricket, mercifully, has two innings' worth of redemption on offer, and the lads took the ball with something to prove. Bowaters, for all our early generosity, could find no answer either, and were bowled out for nought — a scarcely believable turn that had me checking the scorebook twice.

Two capitulations, honours even, and a draw that satisfied precisely no one and everyone in equal measure. John Thirkell continues to lead our run charts for the season with 470, proof that not all was chaos in 1963. Verdict: a match best remembered fondly, described vaguely, and never examined too closely by either side.
