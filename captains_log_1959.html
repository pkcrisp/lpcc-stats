Sun 27 Sep 1959 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformDLDLL

Sunday last, with the sun doing its best to bless proceedings, I won the toss and — in a fit of optimism I may come to regret in print — sent us in to bat. The lads responded handsomely enough: 196 for 8 off our 37 overs, built on a fine 69 from Tony Levick, who sailed serenely past his fifty as though the bowling owed him money. Stan Wratten chipped in a tidy 43 and Pat Hardwick a workmanlike 28, and between them the middle order gave the total a respectability it might not otherwise have earned.

A nod, too, to the history books: Richard Harris's unassuming 7 runs quietly nudged him past Don Hartridge and into 13th on the club's all-time run-scorers list — the kind of milestone that will mean rather more to him in twenty years than it did in the moment. Elsewhere the season's ledgers continue to make interesting reading, with Ray Howard sitting 3rd in the 1959 1st XI charts on 562, Tony Levick leading the Married run count, and John Abnett — modestly, but undeniably — topping the Singles table.

Alas, 196 proved not to be enough, and we were beaten by 45 runs in the end. A batting effort with genuine merit, rather let down elsewhere; we'll regroup, dust ourselves off, and no doubt be better for it next week.

Sat 26 Sep 1959 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDDLLL

Lost the toss on a bright Saturday and found ourselves put in the field — never my favourite way to start a Saturday, but there it is. Dummy Site set about building a total, and while J. Robert Wood, less fortunate, departed for a golden duck. We crept home at 190/7 in 40.3 overs — three wickets to spare and the collective blood pressure of the clubhouse considerably raised.

Roy Clarke had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, 23 byes leaking away rather more than any of us would like, but no matter — the lads got the job done. John Thirkell continues to lead our run charts with 669 for the season, and Terry Whittaker now sits third in the wickets with 41. Two points, thoroughly earned, and a win that owed as much to nerve as to skill.

John Thirkell continues to lead our 1959 run charts on 655, with John Ames second in the wicket-takers on 44.

Beaten by 84 runs, then — a chastening afternoon. Heads up, and back to the nets.

So honours shared, a draw when neither side could force the issue. Pat Hardwick's consistency continues to tell, now fourth in the 1st XI's season charts with 460 runs. A fair result, a fine tea, and, dare I say, rather a good watch.

As it stands, June anniversaries aside, September the sixth found Anchorians CC visiting Linton Park in generous late-summer form, and it must be said the toss did us no favours whatsoever — down went the coin, and down went any hope of batting first. He was quite magnificent, 4-46 of relentless, probing stuff, with MacIldowie miserly alongside him — six maidens, not a run given away lightly — and Howard chipping in gamely for good measure. Ufton and Dixon built the Anchorians' innings sensibly around two well-made fifties, Hart adding useful runs, and 206/9 felt, by tea, like rather a lot to chase.

Chase it we did, after a fashion. Ames, evidently not content with his bowling heroics, top-scored again with 42, Murray anchored things nicely with an unbeaten 31, and Howard weighed in once more with 22 — the lower order clinging on gamely enough to see us to 136/6. Young Buck, meanwhile, endured rather a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, those fifteen byes a cruel statistic that flatters no one and tells its own quiet story.

So a draw it was — honours shared, pride largely intact, and Ames now hauling himself up both the season's bowling and, on this evidence, the batting charts besides. Not a win, but not a defeat either, and on a day when the coin betrayed us early, I'll happily settle for honours even.
