Sun 22 Sep 1991 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformWLWDD

Sunday 22nd September, and Anchorians came to Linton Park to find the toss going against us, which as ever meant an afternoon in the field with hope our chief tactic. They made a fair fist of it too: 215 for 4 off their 41 overs, built largely around Keith Wooding, who batted through for a really rather rude 122 not out, well supported by Graham Siveyer (29) and Ray Willis (22), their second-wicket pair putting on 58 while we tried everything short of asking him nicely to get out. Rob Doick was the pick of our attack with 1 for 29, Jon Harris and Nigel Thirkell chipped in a wicket apiece, and Matt Large bowled two maidens of such stubborn accuracy that Wooding must have wondered whether he'd ever face another bad ball.

Chasing 216, we simply refused to make hard work of it. David Castle and Piers McDonald put on 124 for the first wicket before anyone so much as troubled the scorers with a dismissal, and from there it was really just a question of who got to their fifty first. Castle went on to a fine 62, McDonald made 53, and Peter Whyte added a classy 52 of his own — three half-centuries in a total of 217 for 4, reached with overs to spare.

A thoroughly satisfying six-wicket win, and proof once again that the best response to a lost toss is simply to bowl tidily, bat beautifully, and let the rest take care of itself. Onwards indeed.

Sat 21 Sep 1991 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · HomeformWWLWD

Saturday 21 September 1991, and West Farleigh CC came calling at Linton Park. I won the toss and, with barely a flicker of hesitation, sent us in to bat.

What a platform we built. The openers put on 99 for the first wicket before the breakthrough came, and from there David Castle simply refused to leave, compiling a glorious unbeaten 103. Paul Bowles chipped in handsomely with 62, and Dave Charlton's 38 rounded out a total of 223/3 from our forty overs that had the tea urn rattling with anticipation.

Defending it proved rather more of a grind. Andy Golding gave us an early sniff with a wicket for just 8 on the board, but Golding himself then dug in for 84 and, with Micky Back's 60 alongside him, the pair added a chastening 129 for the second wicket. Dave Latter's 23 kept the total ticking, and D Todd departed for a duck without troubling the scorers, but by the close they'd reached 210/5 from 39 overs. Ian Brattle bowled his heart out for 3-57, with two tidy maidens thrown in, and Paul Bowles backed him up with 2-54 — though poor Roly Edwards had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, twelve byes finding their way past him.

In the end honours were shared, neither side quite able to force the door open. A draw, then — fair reflection of a match that gave rather more than it settled, and no bad way to spend a Saturday.

Sun 15 Sep 1991 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWWLW

Sunday 15 September 1991, and I won the toss against Dummy Site with the sort of confidence that usually precedes disaster — not this time, though. I had no hesitation in batting first, and the lads repaid the faith handsomely: 217 for 3 off our 41 overs, built on a monumental second-wicket stand of 172 between yours truly and Piers McDonald. I'll confess to a certain quiet pride in bringing up a hundred of my own, but Piers's 72 was every bit as vital in getting us to a total that felt, by teatime, thoroughly imposing.

Defending it was another matter. Dummy Site made a proper fist of the chase, J Bolding compiling a fine 52 with useful support from N Twiddy (24) and D Abernathy (22), and an opening stand of 54 that had us shuffling our feet a touch. But Jon Harris turned the afternoon on its head with a superb 6 for 55 — a five-for and thoroughly deserved — while Paul Gibson and Matt Large chipped in with a wicket apiece and Alan Boyns bowled three tidy maidens to keep the squeeze on. Poor N Wood departed for a duck without so much as troubling the scorers. In the end they closed on 158 for 9 off 49, short of our total but stubborn enough to deny us victory.

A word too for Paul Gibson, whose 17 runs eased him past Paul Brattle into 11th on the club's all-time run charts — a nice footnote to the day. So: honours shared, a draw when neither side could force it home. Not the win the scoreline of our innings deserved, but a match to be proud of all the same.

P Martin made a superb hundred, 115 of pure discomfort for us, while M Collingwood purred along to 94 not out and M Foden chipped in an unbeaten 26 to leave them a mighty 245/1 from their 46 overs. Tim Thirkell toiled away for 1-58, a lone voice of resistance against the run tide, and by the close of their innings we were rather glad of the tea interval.

Chasing 246, we began in real style — the openers put on 77 before the first wicket fell, and for a while it looked like a proper game. Mike Elworthy's 44 and Malcolm Pay's 39 kept the innings ticking, and I was pleased enough with my own 51 not out, made the runs that mattered when it counted. A defiant last-wicket stand of 61 gave the total a respectability it probably didn't deserve, and 28 extras did their bit too, taking us to 194/6 from 47 overs — short of the target, but honours were shared in the end.

One line for the scrapbook: Tim's 5 runs eased him past Basil Sears into 5th on the club's all-time run list — quietly historic while everyone else was chasing leather.

Verdict: outgunned with the bat by two magnificent Beverley innings, but a draw earned with pride, and a captain's fifty is never a bad way to spend a Saturday.

A comfortable 64-run win, then, and a thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work all round. Onwards we go.

Five runs. Two points. Thoroughly earned, and thoroughly nerve-shredding — just the way village cricket ought to be.

Sat 07 Sep 1991 🧢 2nd XIv PC Petts Wood CC · HomeformWDLLD

Right, we didn't win. We drew — and honestly, given the mud I'm still finding in my kitbag, honours shared feels about right. Petts Wood came to Linton Park on the 7th of September and gave us a proper afternoon's work.

Roly Edwards top-scored with a gritty 33, Fred Cheesman chipped in with 25, and Malcolm Pay weighed in with 23. Tim Thirkell was superb, prising out four for 43, while Rob Doick bowled with real discipline — three for 30, including three maidens, the sort of spell that has opposition batsmen muttering into their gloves. Colin Bentwood added one for 21. Poor Eddie Sharr departed without troubling the scorers, and at 4 for 1 we dared to dream. But Neil Speers had other ideas, compiling a thoroughly well-made 52 not out to see Petts Wood home to 128/9 off 39 overs and deny us the win our bowling probably deserved.

So, a draw — honours shared, tea presumably lukewarm by the time we got to it. A afternoon of honest toil rather than glory, but with Rob Doick still topping the wicket charts and Dave Todd lurking second in the run charts, the season ticks along nicely enough. Onwards.
