🗓️ 30 Sep – 06 Oct 1972 · 1972 — 1 match

Sat 30 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv BC Bexhill CC · HomeformDLDLW

John Ames set the tone at the toss — I called correctly and never seriously considered doing anything but batting, and by the close of our innings I felt rather vindicated. We finished on a handsome 204/3 from our 34 overs, with John Ames the architect of the innings, motoring past his fifty to 84, well supported by Cliff Anscombe's brisk 45 and John Thirkell's unbeaten 43. A total to make the tea interval taste that bit sweeter.

Bexhill's reply never quite got out of second gear against our attack. They limped to 114/8 from their 46 overs, Martin Grimes (22) and Michael Dawes (20) doing what they could, while poor G Elliott trudged back for a duck. John Thirkell, having already contributed with the bat, did the damage again with the ball, 2-14, while Brian Fuller (2-28, six maides of pure parsimony) and Jon Harris (2-40) kept the squeeze on throughout. I even chipped in with 43 of my own, more through stubbornness than style.

The real headline, though, belonged to John Thirkell, who brought up 16,000 career runs for the club — a landmark that rather puts my own contributions in perspective, and one the tea urn was raised to more than once.

In the end neither side could force matters, and honours were shared. A draw, then — not the result the scoreboard suggests we deserved, but on this form, with Thirkell in irrepressible form and Fuller miserly as ever, we'll take it and look forward to the next one.

🗓️ 23 Sep – 29 Sep 1972 · 1972 — 2 matches

Sun 24 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformDDLDL

We lost the toss on Sunday and were sent in the field, which on a warm September afternoon is rarely the invitation one hopes for. Anchorians settled in and made themselves quite at home, closing on 141/5 from their 46 overs, with T Aitchson unbeaten on 35 and useful support from R Durbridge (29) and G Morris (27). Credit where due — Dick Langridge bowled beautifully for 2-16, Allan Cooper chipped in with 2-35 and five miserly maidens besides, and Nigel Thirkell took 1-21. Poor Cliff Anscombe, meanwhile, had one of those afternoons behind the stumps that goalkeepers dream about forgetting, with 21 byes going begging.

Chasing 142, we made distinctly hard work of it, stumbling to 142/9 off 32.3 overs before getting over the line. John Ames was the mainstay with a fine 46, well supported by Allan Cooper's 24, while Colin Dicker's golden duck rather summed up the nervous energy in the middle order. Still, a win's a win, and by one wicket it doesn't get much tighter than that.

A nod, too, to the record books: Allan Cooper's brace nudged him past Brian Fuller and into 25th on the club's all-time wicket-takers list, while in this season's charts Tim Thirkell sits third with 226 runs and Cooper himself third with 21 wickets. Two points, thoroughly earned, and a night for the lads to sleep soundly — even if Cliff's gloves may need a stern talking-to before next Sunday.

Sat 23 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · AwayformWDDLD

23 September 1972 — Away to West Farleigh CC

Lost the toss, sent in to bowl first, and I confess I rather fancied our chances with the new ball. West Farleigh made us work for every one of their 160, mind — Trevor Back (49) and Micky Back (35) put bat to leather with some purpose, and it took the day's finest performance to prise them out. Brian Fuller was a man transformed, snaring five for 19 — a thoroughly deserved five-for, and one that quietly nudges him up to 25th on the club's all-time wicket-takers list, past the redoubtable Ernest Cruttenden. Ron Hickman toiled away nobly for 2-58 with three miserly maidens, and Paul Bowles chipped in with 1-13. Don Fermor, I. Edwards and John P Day all departed for ducks, which tells its own tale.

Set 161 to win, we mustered 158 all out from 49.3 overs — so close you could taste it, and rather too close for my digestion. Paul Bowles led from the front with a handsome 56, well supported by John Thirkell (32) and Paul Brattle (28); I managed 32 myself, more by luck than judgement. Tim Thirkell and Terry Whittaker, alas, joined the West Farleigh duck club.

Beaten by two runs in the end — the sort of margin that has you replaying every single one on the drive home. Bowles continues to lead our run and wicket charts alike, and Fuller's five-for is a fine consolation. We'll be better for it next week.

🗓️ 16 Sep – 22 Sep 1972 · 1972 — 2 matches

Sun 17 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDWDDL

John Ames strode to the crease with intent that Sunday, and 48 well-timed runs later I could hardly complain about my toss call. Allan Cooper chipped in with a tidy 39 and Dick Langridge saw us home to a respectable 189/7 with an unbeaten 26, though poor John Thirkell will want this innings struck from the record — a golden duck is a lonely walk back to the pavilion. Extras chipped in 26 of their own, which either speaks to some wayward opposition bowling or simply that cricket has a sense of humour.

In the field we set about defending the total, and Ron Hickman was in inspired form, snaring 3-32 with three miserly maidens to show for his afternoon's graft — a spell that, we're reliably informed, edges him past Herbert Rayner into 17th on the club's all-time wicket-takers' list, which is the sort of statistic that keeps a man warm on a cold boundary. Allan Cooper and Tony Gauna picked up a wicket apiece, and between them the bowlers kept the opposition to 95/5 from 21.4 overs, Batchelor and Jeanes doing most of the damage with the bat. Eight byes, mind, told their own tale behind the stumps.

As for your correspondent, I too made a duck, and the less said about that the better. Season honours continue to accrue elsewhere — Tim Thirkell third in the run charts, Allan Cooper third among the wicket-takers.

In the end, honours shared and a draw that satisfied nobody and offended nobody — which, on reflection, is about as English an outcome as cricket allows.

Sat 16 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv CA Charles Arkcoll · HomeformLDWDD

Saturday last, we welcomed Charles Arkcoll to Linton Park under grey but obliging skies, and promptly lost the toss — which, as ever, meant leather before willow for us. No complaints; the lads settled into the field with purpose, and Charles Arkcoll were shepherded out for 86 in 29.3 overs, Brian Aiston (25) and Keith Filmer (20) doing the heavy lifting for the visitors while wickets tumbled around them, John Mighall and Vic Wellard both back in the pavilion without troubling the scorer. The afternoon belonged, unquestionably, to Terry Donovan, whose 5-16 was as fine a spell as we've seen this season and thoroughly deserved. Tony Gauna toiled away for 3-34 with four maidens to his name — not a run given away lightly, that man — and Nigel Thirkell chipped in with 2-2, tidy as you like.

Alas, 86 proved beyond us. We subsided to 67 all out in 34.2 overs, our batting never quite finding its rhythm, and poor Mike Iliffe's afternoon summed up by a duck. Nineteen runs short at the death — a margin that stings rather more than the scorecard perhaps suggests, given the bowling that preceded it.

Elsewhere, the season ticks on nicely: Tim Thirkell remains third in the 1st XI run charts with 212, and Tony Gauna fourth among the wicket-takers with 18. As for Saturday — a fine bowling display let down by the bat. We'll dust ourselves off and come back stronger next week.

🗓️ 09 Sep – 15 Sep 1972 · 1972 — 1 match

Sun 10 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv BC Bexhill CC · AwayformLLDWD

Sunday 10 September saw us motor over to Bexhill CC, coin duly won and battle plan set: bat first and see what the square would give us.

It gave us 150 for 4 off our 49 overs, most of it courtesy of Paul Bowles, who middled his way to 90 not out and, en route, brought up 4,000 career runs for the club — a landmark worth more than one round at the bar. Robert Chance will not want reminded of his contribution, back in the pavilion for a duck before he'd troubled the scorers, but these things happen to the best of us.

In the field we squeezed Bexhill to 147 for 8 from 36.2 overs, J Wisden's 40 the main resistance, with Neil Harding (29) and X Stephens (25) chipping in round him. Tony Gauna was the pick with 3 for 34, Paul Bowles backed up his batting with 3 for 49, and Ron Hickman took 2 for 38, while Allan Cooper strangled the scoring with four maidens and Martyn Cowley, A Hopkins and Robert Saxby all departed for ducks of their own. I managed a modest 19 with the bat, more supporting cast than leading man.

In the end honours were shared, neither side able to force it home. A fair result, and with Bowles now sitting proudly atop both our run and wicket charts for the season, a very good day to be his captain.

🗓️ 02 Sep – 08 Sep 1972 · 1972 — 2 matches

Sun 03 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformDLLDW

Here's the match report:

There are moments in a captain's life when losing the toss feels like a mercy, and Sunday's trip to Staplehurst was one such occasion — though quite what I was being spared from became clear soon enough. Put in to bowl, we watched C Rogers make merry with 89 well-constructed runs, with A West (30 not out) and P. Holland (29) lending sturdy support as Staplehurst rattled up 201 for 8 from their 49.4 overs. Credit where due, though: Paul Bowles was in devastating touch, spinning his way to 5 for 72 and accounting for both J Munn and B Pudney without troubling the scorers, while Ron Hickman chipped in with 2 for 62 and Tony Gauna bowled three overs so tight you couldn't have posted a letter through them.

Chasing 202, we made a decent fist of it without ever quite threatening to get there, closing on 166 for 8 from our 43. Bowles, evidently not content with his bowling heroics, top-scored with a splendid 68, ably assisted by Allan Cooper (31) and John Thirkell (27); I managed a workmanlike 27 myself, while poor Benny Bell's afternoon began and ended without a run to his name.

There was, at least, a footnote worth savouring amid the toil — Bowles's five-wicket haul nudged him past Dick Langridge into seventh on the club's all-time wicket-takers' list, a achievement that will doubtless be toasted, however modestly, in the bar.

A draw, then — honours shared, pride largely intact, and a long drive home to reflect on what might have been.

Sat 02 Sep 1972 🧢 1st XIv Whitstable CC · AwayformDDLLD

Whitstable, 2 September 1972 — and never let it be said we do things the easy way.

Lost the toss, put out to field, and after Whitstable had crawled to 87 all out in 32.5 overs I confess a certain smugness set in. Rightly so, perhaps: Graham Mountford bowled like a man possessed, 6 for 42 and a five-for thoroughly earned, ably supported by Tony Gauna (2-19) and the ever-parsimonious Allan Cooper, whose 3 maidens on the way to 1 for 23 told their own story. A- Collinson's 26 was the only real resistance the home side could muster.

Then, as is traditional at Linton Park, we set about making a straightforward chase look like open-heart surgery. Wakeford, Tritton and Nutten all departed without troubling the scorers, and poor Jon Harris contributed the golden variety of nothing, but Allan Cooper — not content with his heroics in the field — dug in for 38 to drag us, wheezing and stumbling, over the line by two wickets with 88 for 8 from 36.5 overs. A committee-room finish if ever there was one.

Small mercies and larger footnotes: Cooper's solitary wicket nudged him past Ernest Cruttenden into 25th on the club's all-time list, and he now sits third in this season's bowling table with 18 — Andrew Moss, still imperious with 363 runs, remains out on his own at the top. All told, a scrappy, nervy, thoroughly satisfying afternoon. We'll take it.

🗓️ 26 Aug – 01 Sep 1972 · 1972 — 2 matches

Wed 30 Aug 1972 🧢 Midweek XIv Gravesend CC · HomeformLDDDL

Lost the toss again on Wednesday, which by now feels less like bad luck and more like a personal vendetta from whatever coin the umpires keep in their pocket. Gravesend chose to bat, and we trooped into the field wondering what sort of afternoon lay in store. As it turned out, rather a good one. P Rue battled to a fine 52, but around him the innings crumbled agreeably, Mick Kingston, Tom Church and Ian Brattle all shipping out without so much as a nibble at the scorebook. Paul Bowles was quite magnificent, 6 for 36 off a miserly seven maidens, with Dick Langridge (3-15) and Nigel Thirkell (1-19) mopping up the rest. All out 113 in 42.2 overs — a total that felt gettable, if we could only bat like grown-ups.

We very nearly didn't. Andrew Moss played the innings of the day, 41 well-made runs that also nudged him past George Champion into 14th on the club's all-time run-scoring list, with Alan Crowest chipping in a handy 33. Ray Grayland's golden duck rather summed up the rest of the order, and 21 byes suggested our man behind the stumps had a testing afternoon of his own. Still, 117 for 5 in 25.5 overs got us home by five wickets. Dick Langridge's three scalps, meanwhile, lifted him past Paul Bowles into 7th on the all-time wicket-takers' list — a fine week for the record books. Two points, thoroughly earned, and a result to be quietly smug about at the bar.

Sun 27 Aug 1972 🧢 Sunday NVKCv TC Troon CC · AwayformLWWWW

Sunday 27 August 1972 saw us make the trip to Troon, and for once the coin behaved itself — heads, and into the middle we went.

We batted first and got ourselves to a respectable 167/9 from our forty overs, built chiefly on a fine 48 from Paul Bowles and 45 from Cliff Anscombe, with Andrew Moss chipping in a handy 25. Not everyone's day at the crease, mind — John Thirkell, Graham Mountford and Ron Hickman all made that lonely walk back for nought, three ducks in one innings being rather more than the club average, and rather more than any of them will thank me for mentioning.

Troon, however, had other ideas. J Spry and B Carter simply declined to get out, cruising to unbeaten fifties of 88 and 74 respectively and seeing their side home at 171/1 in just 35.2 overs — a stand that made our bowling figures look rather more generous than we'd have liked. Graham Mountford was our pick of the attack with 1-39, and Allan Cooper at least kept things tight with two maidens. As for my own contribution, a duck, gracefully drawn under a veil and never spoken of again.

There was a silver lining higher up: Paul Bowles remains top of the 1972 Sunday NVKC run charts with 237, and Graham Mountford sits second in the wickets with 11. Not our day on the field, though — beaten by nine wickets, heads up, and back to the nets.

Sunday 20 August 1972, and a rare treat: home advantage, a good toss to win, and no earthly reason not to bat first. Sat 19 Aug 1972 🧢 1st XIv BC Bowaters CC · AwayformWDDDL

We travelled to Bowaters on Saturday 19 August full of quiet optimism, and having called correctly at the toss I didn't so much as pause before opting to bat — home advantage may have been theirs, but the toss, for once, was ours. We laboured to 150/8 from our 44.3 overs, a total that owed everything to the middle order rolling their sleeves up: Tim Thirkell and Nigel Thirkell both made tidy 28s, with Basil Sears chipping in a useful 24. It was not, in the end, a good day. Terry Donovan toiled manfully for his 1-22, two maidens included, with M- Flint chipping in at 1-32, but with only two wickets to show for 35 overs between bat and gloves, the game had rather slipped away from us. My own afternoon behind the stumps was a chastening one — 12 byes is not a number I'll be dwelling on with any fondness.

Small consolations: Basil Sears remains third in the season's run charts on 194, and Terry Donovan sits fifth among the wicket-takers with ten. But an eight-wicket defeat is an eight-wicket defeat, and no amount of table-gazing softens it. We'll dust ourselves down and be better for it next week.

In the end Sennocke closed on 180/5, five runs shy and one wicket to spare — honours, quite fairly, shared. A draw that satisfied nobody and disappointed nobody, which is, I suppose, cricket's way of telling us both sides deserved better.

Sat 05 Aug 1972 🧢 1st XIv ? · AwayformLDLWD

Off to face for the lads on Saturday 5th August 1972.

Winning the toss (or perhaps losing it, history is silent on the matter), we were sent into the field, and spent the best part of the afternoon watching our visitors compile 189 all out from 45.5 overs — a total that owed much to Pete Worsnop's well-made 63, with useful support from Stuckey (47) and Grainger (20).
