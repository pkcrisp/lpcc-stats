Sat 29 Sep 1990 🧢 1st XIv HC Hastings & St Leonards Priory CC · HomeformWWDWD

Saturday last, up against Hastings & St Leonards Priory CC, I won the toss and didn't dither for a second — batting first on a day like that is not so much a decision as a formality. And how the lads repaid the faith. We raced along to 63 for the openers before the first wicket went down, and from there Piers McDonald simply refused to be parted from the crease, compiling a glorious 127 not out — three figures, no less, and made to look rather easier than it surely was. Peter Whyte chipped in nicely with 26, and the innings found its real backbone in a stand of 73 for the third wicket. We declared our efforts, so to speak, at 212 for 3 from 43 overs, which felt like a total to be proud of and slightly nervous about in equal measure.

Defending it, the visitors made a watchful start, ten for one from their three overs, with Matt Large the pick of our attack at 1 for 8 — economical enough to suggest we might have sneaked it, had the overs allowed.

A small note of pride amid the day's play: Tim Thirkell's 18 runs eased him past David Castle and up to eighth on the club's all-time run-scorers list — these things matter more than they ought to, and quite right too.

In the end neither side could force the issue, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but on this evidence, no bad afternoon's cricket.

Wed 26 Sep 1990 🧢 Midweek XIv Penshurst Park CC · AwayformWWLWL

Won the toss at Penshurst Park on Wednesday and, in a rare fit of decisiveness, stuck us in to bat — a call that for once didn't have the committee reaching for the sherry in despair. A note for the scrapbook: Jon Harris sailed past 1700 career wickets, a landmark achieved with his usual air of mild indifference. A fine win by 98 runs, then — onwards, and rather pleased with ourselves.

Sun 23 Sep 1990 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformLWWDW

Sunday 23rd September saw us welcome Anchorians CC, and having called correctly at the toss I had no hesitation whatsoever in sending us in to bat — a decision I stand by in principle, if not entirely in outcome. What followed was twenty overs of what I might charitably call "character-building" batting, the innings folding for 69 all out. It was not, I confess, a total to strike fear into anyone, and there were one or two shots played that will be quietly revisited over a pint before they're revisited again in the retelling.

Credit where it's due, though — Peter Whyte stood firm throughout, carrying his bat for a splendid 36 not out while wickets fell around him like autumn leaves. Without him we'd have been discussing something considerably more embarrassing than 69, and the club owes him a round for services rendered.

With such modest fuel in the tank, containing Anchorians became the order of the day, and to the lads' great credit that's precisely what we did — a draw secured through grit, guile, and no small measure of stubbornness in the field.

Elsewhere in the season, it's worth noting Paul Gibson continues his fine form with the bat, sitting fourth in the 1990 first XI run charts on 591 — a reminder that not everything this season has been quite so scratchy.

Verdict: a scrappy 69 rescued by Whyte's defiance and a team effort in the field — no classic, but a draw earned is a defeat avoided, and we'll gladly take it.

Sat 15 Sep 1990 🧢 1st XIv SC Southborough CC · AwayformDLWLW

It's Southborough away, back in September 1990. Not a fixture I'll forget in a hurry.

There's no sign of an opposition club side by that name in cricket circles I'd trust myself to invent details about — but that's fine, I don't need to invent anything. Let me write the report from exactly the facts given.

Losing the toss on a Southborough morning is never how you'd script it, but there are worse fates than being asked to bowl first, and so it proved. Southborough huffed and puffed their way to 169, with only Chris Bass playing anything like an innings for 46, and a sixth-wicket stand of 65 threatening for a spell to make a proper match of it. Paul Spencer, mercifully for us, never got that far — back in the hutch without so much as a single to his name. It was Matt Large who did the real damage, turning the ball square for 5-28 with eight maidens thrown in for good measure — the sort of miserly, mesmerising spell that has opposition batsmen playing for the Sunday roast rather than the scoreboard. Sean Charlton (3-20) and Jon Harris (1-27) tidied up the rest.

A target of 170 should hold few terrors, and so it proved, though not without the odd hiccup — Dave Charlton's golden duck chief among them, and poor Peter Whyte having a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, 23 byes leaking through like the Southborough drainage. But Paul Gibson (64 not out) and Brian Piper (53 not out) put together the innings of the day, a watchful second-wicket stand of 59 setting the platform, with Nigel Thirkell's 29 and my own modest 29 filling in the gaps. We cantered home by seven wickets with overs to spare.

A thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work, and with Thirkell and Large continuing to top the season's charts for runs and wickets respectively, the engine room of this side is purring nicely. Onwards indeed.
