🗓️ 26 Sep – 02 Oct 1953 · 1953 — 1 match

Sun 27 Sep 1953 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLD

Sunday, 27 September 1953, and Linton Park travelled with the toss to win — which I duly did, electing to bat, and I still maintain it was the right call, whatever the scorebook might later suggest about that decision.

Our innings, in truth, was a story told almost entirely by one man. William Buck stood firm for an unbeaten 26, playing the shepherd to a flock that otherwise scattered rather too readily. The rest of us contributed what might charitably be called moral support: Tony Tipples and John Ames both made the long walk back for ducks, and I'm afraid your correspondent must own up to a third — a captain's innings lasting roughly as long as it takes to say "middle and leg." The less said the better, though I note the club ledger will say it for me regardless, in stark and unforgiving digits.

For all that, William's knock was enough to keep him top of the 1953 Married run charts, a small but genuine consolation amid the wreckage, and proof that one steady head can carry a great deal of collective folly.

In the end neither side could force home advantage, and honours were shared — a draw that, given the state of our top order, felt less like stalemate and more like a merciful escape. We live to bat again, preferably with rather more application next time.

🗓️ 19 Sep – 25 Sep 1953 · 1953 — 2 matches

Sun 20 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformLDLLW

Skipper's Log — Sunday 20 September 1953

Won the toss, and there really was only one call to make: bat. On a day when 185 felt like it might just be enough, Tony Levick played the innings of the afternoon, 63 not out and unbeaten at the close, with Stan Wratten giving him fine company for a well-made fifty of his own. 185/6 from our 48 overs seemed a total to be proud of as we walked off for tea.

And for a while it looked like it might be. E Watson, G Ashby and L Brunger trooped back without so much as a run to show for it, and Tony Snell was in menacing form — 3 for 8, with nine maidens that told their own story of a bowler in complete command. Alan Wilkes chipped in handsomely with 3 for 27, and Michael Morton with 2 for 3, and between them they had the opposition all out for 58. Poor Tony Levick had a torrid time of it behind the stumps, mind, with ten byes going begging — the one blemish on an otherwise gleaming afternoon.

There was cause for real celebration too: Alan Wilkes brought up his fiftieth career wicket, and both he and Levick moved up the club's all-time lists in the same match. Quite how, with all that in our favour, we contrived to lose by 127 runs is a mystery I shall be pondering over my tea and toast this week. Chin up, lads — we'll be better for it next Sunday.

Sat 19 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv Bearsted CC · HomeformLLDLL

Saturday 19th September 1953, and Bearsted CC came to Linton Park under blue skies and lost the toss argument on our behalf — they batted, we bowled, and thank heavens for it. Seventy-two all out in 27.3 overs is not a total to trouble a scorer's pencil for long, and though A 'Tony' Snook threatened to make a proper afternoon of it with 25, the rest of the Bearsted order rather lost the plot, tumbling from 57-3 to 61-6 as if the tea urn had been mentioned. David Sayer, Gordon A Hawkins and Mick Gibson all fell without so much as a run to their name at the crease — a trio of ducks to warm the heart of any fielding captain. Alan Wilkes was quite magnificent, ripping through them with 8-39, while Gerald Wright bowled with the miserly discipline of a man counting out his own pension, seven maidens and 2-25 to show for it.

Chasing 73, we made rather harder work of it than the target deserved. Tom Osborn's watchful 25 and John Thirkell's 21 had us cruising nicely, and I contributed 21 of my own, but from 72-4 to 76-7 the whole enterprise wobbled alarmingly, with Richard Harris, Alan Wilkes and Gerald Wright all making the long walk back for nought. Still, we got there — won by 0 wickets, which is cricket's way of reminding you that a win by the skin of your teeth counts every bit as much as one by a mile.

Two points, thoroughly earned, and a chastening reminder that we do enjoy making things difficult for ourselves.

🗓️ 12 Sep – 18 Sep 1953 · 1953 — 2 matches

Sun 13 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformWLLDL

A grey Sunday, the thirteenth of September, and Dummy Site rolled up to Linton Park with the coin falling their way as it so often does when it matters. They chose to bat, and we trooped out into the field with more hope than expectation. They ground their way to 173 for 7 off 43.1 overs, G- Lukehurst top-scoring with 49 and K- Walker unbeaten on 48, R- Harris chipping in 30. Small mercies: W- Cleaves went for a duck at our hands, and Stan Wratten was in inspired form with 2 for 3, backed up nicely by Gordon Fenner (2-24) and John Ames (2-46). Alan Wilkes, meanwhile, bowled as if runs were rationed, three maidens and not an inch given away.

With the bat we set about our reply with real purpose, reaching 176 for 4 in 31.3 overs. I'll confess to a quiet pride in top-scoring myself with 51 not out, but the real backbone came from Tom Osborn's 46 and William Buck's 35. Poor William Blight had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, nine byes slipping through fingers that are usually far more reliable.

There was cheer amid the toil, though: Tom's innings lifted him past Rev F A Leveson-Gower to 18th on the club's all-time run list, and Alan's solitary wicket edged him past W H Fryer into 23rd among our bowlers — with William Buck (856) and Alan (39) still driving the 1953 charts.

All told, though, we finished undone by six wickets — a day for the nets, not the scorebook.

Sat 12 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformLWLLD

Saturday 12th September saw Dummy Site visit Linton Park, and after losing the toss — no great surprise, my coin-calling remains a work in progress — we were put into the field. Not that we minded. Our openers, F Hooker and H Davidson, made short work of the top order in spirit if not in figures, and the innings folded for a distinctly modest 58 off 26 overs. The afternoon belonged to Alan Wilkes, whose 6-33 was a five-for and richly, richly deserved; Michael Morton offered superb support with 4-11 and six maidens, bowling with the kind of miserly discipline that leaves batsmen talking to themselves.

With 58 to chase, we set about it in no great hurry, closing on 135/3 from 24 overs. William Buck top-scored with a handsome 44, Basil Sears chipped in with 30, and John Thirkell saw us home unbeaten on 21. I managed 21 myself, which I shall claim as a captain's innings and leave it at that. Bob Arnold had a wretched time behind the stumps, leaking 12 byes — the sort of afternoon best forgotten, though I daresay he won't be allowed to.

Small print for the scorebook: Wilkes's haul lifted him past Robert Muir to 24th on the club's all-time wicket list, while Buck and Wilkes continue to lead the season's run and wicket charts respectively. A comfortable win, and thoroughly deserved — the sort of performance that reminds us why we bother turning up on a Saturday.

Sun 06 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv GK Gillingham CC, Kent · HomeformDLWLL

From the captain's chair — Sunday 06 September 1953. We hosted Gillingham CC, Kent, and lost the toss for our trouble; they elected to bat, and we trudged out to bowl on what looked a decent shirt-sleeves afternoon for it.

Gillingham made a tidy 210/9 from their 43.1 overs, built patiently around G Veroest's unbeaten 32, with A Tansley (29) and O Barden (25) chipping in handsomely. It could have been worse but for Alan Wilkes, who bowled with real purpose to return 5-57 — four maidens in there too, not a man to be taken lightly — while Stan Wratten and Tony Snell picked up a wicket apiece to keep things honest.

Our reply was rather more stop-start. William Buck batted with real application for 43, and Stan Wratten (27) and John Thirkell (20) gave him company, but we subsided to 129/8 from our 36 overs and had to settle for the draw. I contributed a workmanlike 20 myself, while poor Tony Levick had an afternoon to forget behind the stumps — 17 byes is not a figure one enters into the book with any pride. Spare a thought too for Richard Harris and Gordon Fenner, both back in the pavilion without troubling the scorers — the Duck Club welcomes two new members.

Still, William Buck now sits proudly atop the season's run charts with 777, and Alan Wilkes leads the wickets with 32 — the engine room of our summer. Honours shared, then, and honestly, on the day, a draw felt like the fairest result going.

Sat 05 Sep 1953 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · AwayformDDLWL

Saturday 5 September 1953 saw us take the road out to Dummy Site, and for once the coin obliged me — I called correctly and, with the sort of confidence that only deserts a captain later in the day, sent us in to bat.

It proved a sound enough call, if not a chanceless one. Still, 157 gave us something to bowl at, and bowl at it we did. Their reply folded for a mere 56 in 19.4 overs, a- Lewis and R- Latter both back in the pavilion without scoring. Gerald Wright was the architect of the collapse, a thing of real beauty with 6-31 and three miserly maidens thrown in for good measure, Alan Wilkes offering fine support with 3-21. Your correspondent, meanwhile, contributed a duck of his own with the bat — we shall move swiftly on.

There was a happy footnote amid the carnage: Gerald's six-for nudged him past Tony Snell into 18th on the club's all-time wicket-takers list. A day, then, of contrasts — batting that stuttered, bowling that sang, and a result comfortably in our favour. Verdict: a fine team performance, and one Gerald in particular can be very proud of.

Sat 29 Aug 1953 🧢 1st XIv HC Hunton Wanderers CC · AwayformLDDLW

Saturday 29 August 1953, away to Hunton Wanderers CC, and the coin let us down before a ball was bowled — they won the toss and chose to bat, leaving us in the field first. Toil we did, though not without reward: A Pilcher compiled a fine 65, well supported by Reg Oldfield's 47, before Hunton were eventually bowled out. R Murray fell for a duck to our bowling, and Alan Wilkes was the chief tormentor with 4-68, J Barclay chipping in with 3-48 and Gerald Wright a tidy 1-17.

Then came our reply, and here the tale turns considerably sadder. We were bowled out for just 31 in 16.4 overs, a collapse comprehensive enough to fill the Duck Club ledger nicely — John Thirkell, N Simmons and Bob Arnold all walked back without troubling the scorer, and your correspondent, I'm mortified to report, joined them. Eleven byes told their own story of a testing afternoon behind the stumps for William Blight.

One crumb of comfort amid the wreckage: Gerald Wright's solitary wicket carried him past W H Fryer to 22nd on the club's all-time list — no small achievement in a grim week, and it keeps him third in this season's wicket charts with 25, with Basil Sears fifth among the run-makers on 243.

A defeat by 140 runs, then, and no dressing it up. Heads up, lads. Back to the nets.
