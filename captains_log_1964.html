🗓️ 26 Sep – 02 Oct 1964 · 1964 — 2 matches

Sun 27 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv Linton Park CC · HomeformDWWDL

Sunday 27th September saw us welcome Linton Park CC in glorious end-of-season fashion, and winning the toss I elected to bat — the right call, I felt, though the lads might argue that point once they'd seen us bowled out for 135 in the fortieth over. Alan Tucker led the way with a tidy 28, well supported by Stan Wratten's 23, and I managed to contribute 23 of my own before getting out in whatever manner captains generally do — nobly, I like to think. Poor Robert Murray, however, will not be dining out on this innings; a golden duck is a cruel thing to carry home on the bus.

There was, mercifully, a bright spot amid the rubble: Terry Whittaker brought up 1,000 career runs for the club, a landmark that deserves rather more applause than our total did. Elsewhere in the season's ledger, Fred Greenfield continues to churn out runs and sits second in the 1st XI charts on 399, while John Thirkell leads the 2nd XI standings with 56 — proof that somewhere in this club, someone is in form.

As for the chase, Linton Park strolled home by seven wickets with the minimum of fuss, leaving us to trudge off rather sheepishly. Not our finest hour, and no amount of tea and cake could quite disguise it. Heads up, though — back to the nets, and no doubt we'll have the bat swinging truer next week.

Sat 26 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformDDWWD

Won the toss on a bright late-September afternoon and, in a fit of what I'd like to call confidence rather than madness, stuck us in first. It didn't entirely repay the faith — 149 all out in 38.1 overs, with only Gordon Fenner giving the scorebook anything to admire, top-scoring with 35. Poor Len Conway and Dick Langridge both trudged back for ducks, joining the club's less illustrious list, while I contributed a workmanlike 17 that nobody will remember by teatime.

With the ball we gave a decent account of ourselves without quite landing the killer blow. The Anchorians replied with 150 for 6 off 47 overs, built chiefly around P. Woolsey's classy 60 and useful support from J. Cavey's 41 — their third-wicket stand of 62 was the platform that did for us. Credit where due, though: we did have them wobbling, 122 for 3 collapsing to 125 for 6 as A. Hart and S. Harris both departed without scoring. John Ames was the pick of our attack with 3 for 21, Dick Langridge chipped in with 2 for 41 and three tidy maidens, though poor Roy Clarke behind the stumps had a torrid time of it with 12 byes conceded.

Not our day, then — beaten by four wickets, with John Thirkell (492 runs) and Dick Langridge (43 wickets) still flying the flag atop the season's charts. Heads up, chins out, back to the nets we go.

Sun 20 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformWDDWW

Sunday last saw us host our visitors at Linton Park in what turned into a proper old-fashioned tussle, and when the coin came down my way I had no hesitation in asking us to bat first on a pitch that looked, and largely played, a treat.

Runs came steadily rather than in a torrent. Fred Greenfield led the charge with a fine 59, well past his fifty, while John Thirkell — not for the first time this season — batted with real composure for an unbeaten 42, and Terry Whittaker chipped in with a brisk 31. I managed a modest 42 myself, more through good fortune than technique, and we closed on a healthy 221 for 7 from our 43.2 overs.

With the ball, it was Terry Whittaker's afternoon. Two wickets for 21, four maidens, and barely a boundary to be had off him — the sort of spell that makes captaincy look easy. Dick Langridge (2-44) and Basil Sears (1-12) chipped in nicely too, and poor Prout departed without so much as a run to his name. G. Winter's 37 held their innings together, ably assisted by D. Pound's 30 and Maskell's 25, but they could only reach 151 for 8 from their 45 overs — leaving them well short but, alas, not quite chaseable enough for a result either way.

So honours shared, a draw that satisfied nobody and disappointed nobody. With Thirkell topping our run charts and Whittaker running away with the wickets, the season has a healthy look to it — and a point apiece will do us no harm at all.

Sat 19 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv MC Maidstone YMCA CC · HomeformLWDDW

Saturday, 19 September 1964. Home to Maidstone Ymca CC, and the coin let me down again — they had first use of both toss and bat, though as it turned out that suited us rather nicely.

They mustered 107 all out in 35.4 overs, Phillips top-scoring with 33 and Smith chipping in 21, but the middle and lower order simply evaporated — Crabtree, Robinson and Merricks all back in the pavilion without a run to show for it as we slid them from 101-6 to 102-9 in the blink of an eye. The architect of the collapse was Alan Tucker, spinning a web of 7-21 with five maidens thrown in for good measure — the sort of spell that has opposition batsmen muttering into their tea. Terry Donovan (2-27) and Ron Hickman (1-15) tidied up the rest between them. I confess our 9 byes suggest Roy Clarke behind the stumps had rather a testing afternoon, but nobody was going to mention it after a display like that.

Chasing 108, Terry Whittaker played the innings of the day, an unbeaten 74 built on good sense and better timing, with Basil Sears keeping him company on 26 not out as we cantered home to 110 without loss in just 24 overs. Ten wickets, no fuss, home before the pub shut.

A thoroughly good day for the club — the bowlers did the damage, the openers did the tidying up, and I merely had to stand at slip and enjoy the show.

Sun 13 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv SC Staplehurst CC · AwayformDLWDD

We finished second at the toss table on Sunday against Staplehurst, and having called it right I sent us in – a decision I still stand by, even after we contrived to lose our first wicket for just one run. From there Joe Fenner (32) and John Thirkell (30) did the steadying, dragging us up to a defendable if unspectacular 126 all out. Special mention, of the wooden-spoon variety, to poor Basil Sears, who contributed his golden duck to proceedings with the sort of dignity only club cricket demands.

Out in the field, though, we were a different proposition entirely. Staplehurst never looked like chasing it down, subsiding to 77 all out with P- Hollands and H- Catt both back in the pavilion without scoring. The architect of the collapse was Len Conway, spinning his way to a magnificent 6-29 with eight maidens thrown in for good measure – the sort of miserly, metronomic spell that makes batsmen question their life choices. Terry Whittaker and Alan Tucker chipped in with a wicket apiece, and I even managed a tidy little 30 with the bat myself, which I shall not let anyone forget.

A 49-run win, two points banked, and a thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work. With John Thirkell still leading our run charts and Terry Whittaker topping the wickets column for the season, the engine room of this side is purring nicely. Staplehurst, I suspect, will not be too sorry to see the back of us.

We won the toss, and I did the only sensible thing a captain can do on a fine September Saturday: I stuck us in to bat. Eight byes conceded behind the stumps suggested our gloveman had earned one too.

In the end honours were shared, and rightly so — a draw that satisfied nobody and offended nobody, which is very often the truest result in this funny old game.

Wed 09 Sep 1964 🧢 Midweek XIv BC Birchington CC · AwayformWLLLL

We got over the line by 42 runs at Birchington on the ninth of September, and I'll confess my toss call was one of the easier decisions I've made in seventeen summers of captaincy — bat first, and let the lads have their say with the willow. Cliff Anscombe led from the front with a well-made 78, and Bob Brigden wasn't far behind with 54 of his own; the two of them, alongside William Buck's tidy 30, hauled us up to a healthy 204/7 off 43.4 overs, with a third-wicket stand of 66 doing the heavy lifting.

Defending it looked a formality on paper, but Birchington had other ideas. M Elgar's 53 and useful contributions from K Williams and R Hobcraft, plus a stubborn 55-run third-wicket stand, dragged them back into it after a shaky start at 5 for the first wicket. It was Terry Whittaker who did for them in the end — four wickets, four maidens, miserly as you like — with Charles Crellin and I chipping in two apiece, and E Diaz departing without troubling the scorers off my bowling.

A tidy afternoon for me personally with four wickets, and a rather special one for Terry, whose four scalps see him climb to fourth on the club's all-time wicket-takers list — no mean feat. Cliff, meanwhile, continues to lead our Midweek run and wicket charts, greedy so-and-so that he is. All told, a proper day's cricket, won the hard way, and precisely the sort of afternoon that makes the long drive home feel a good deal shorter.

Sun 06 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv HC Harrietsham CC · HomeformWLDLW

William Buck's day, unquestionably — a captain barely needs to write a report when his number three bats through for 113 not out, but here we are, and someone has to sing his praises properly.

Buck was serene throughout, ably shepherded by Robert Murray's unbeaten fifty (59) and a tidy 24 from Fred Greenfield lower down. A total that left our openers with feet up in the pavilion rather longer than most Sundays.

They crawled home to 134-8, honours shared. Fred Greenfield's ten byes suggest the ball did a bit behind the stumps too — a note for training, perhaps.

Records tumbled quietly amid it all: Buck now second on the club's all-time run list, Conway up to fourteenth among the wicket-takers. A draw, then — but one that felt, in truth, rather more ours than theirs.

Sat 05 Sep 1964 🧢 1st XIv SC St Lukes Maidstone CC · HomeformDWLDL

Saturday 5th September 1964, and St Lukes Maidstone rolled up to Linton Park with the sun out and, rather more importantly, the toss in their pocket. They chose to bat, which after the day's events I can only put down to blind optimism or a genuine misreading of my bowling attack.

They made a fair fist of it early on — 71-1 before the first wicket fell, C. Pearson and J. Beesley building steadily towards 132/9 off their 39 overs. Then John Thirkell decided enough was enough. He spun through them like a man late for his tea, taking 5-30 with two maidens thrown in for good measure, and suddenly we'd gone from mild concern to 116-4 and then 121-7 in what felt like the blink of an eye. Colin Eldred (2-31) and Terry Donovan (2-38) tidied up the rest with the sort of quiet efficiency that never quite makes the headlines but always wins matches.

Chasing 133, we made harder work of it than the bowling perhaps deserved, crawling home at 133/6 from 35.1 overs. Fred Greenfield led the way with 41, Robert Murray chipped in with 25, and Colin Eldred — clearly not content with his afternoon's bowling — added an unbeaten 23, the 55-run third-wicket stand the difference between comfort and calamity.

Two points, four wickets, and a Thirkell masterclass to remember. Thoroughly earned, and I shan't apologise for the celebratory tea.

William Buck's hundred at Linton Park.

A thoroughly satisfying day's work, then, and John Thirkell and Terry Whittaker continue to lead our season's run and wicket charts respectively — the engine room is in fine order. Verdict: a proper win, and a timely reminder that when the ball's doing the talking, we can be a hard side to face.

Terry Donovan and Barry Langridge, less happily, took the long walk back for nought apiece — the Duck Club welcomes two new members.

We scrambled home 123/7 from 39 overs, winners by three wickets. Two points, hard-earned, and a result that flattered our comfort rather more than our batting deserved.
