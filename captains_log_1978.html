Sun 01 Oct 1978 🧢 1st XIv Linton Park CC · HomeformDWLWW

John Ames had said before the game that he fancied a bat if the toss fell our way, and for once the cricketing gods listened to me rather than him — I called correctly and had no hesitation in padding us up first on a Sunday that felt made for batting. It didn't quite turn into the run-feast the morning promised, but 148 for 9 off our 32 overs was a total built on real substance rather than fireworks. Ames led from the front with a classy 56, and he had sturdy company in Brian Woodcock, who eased past his own half-century to finish on 55 — between them they did the bulk of the damage. Terry Donovan chipped in an unbeaten 34 to see us home to a defendable score, of the sort that asks bowling sides some serious questions.

There was history in the air too. Alan Wilkes turned out for his 100th appearance in Linton colours, a landmark that deserves rather more fanfare than a line in the scorebook, and hearty congratulations are due. Mike Iliffe, meanwhile, quietly nudged his way past Robert Chance and into 15th on the club's all-time run-scorers list with a tidy 21 — the sort of milestone that sneaks up on you mid-innings. Add in Nigel Thirkell sitting third in this season's 1st XI charts on 316 runs, and Martin Mears leading the 2nd XI's with 52, and it's plain the runs are flowing all over the club this year.

In the end neither side could quite force the door open, and honours were shared. A draw, then — not the story we'd have written, but with landmarks like Alan's, nobody left Linton Park feeling short-changed.

Sat 30 Sep 1978 🧢 1st XIv Lordswood CC · HomeformDDWLW

Saturday 30 September 1978, and Lordswood CC rolled up as our final visitors of the season. I lost the toss — no great surprise, my coin-calling has never troubled the record books — and we were put in the field first.

The lads did us proud with the ball. Lordswood ground their way to 142/6 off 49 overs, Colin Gardener the chief architect with a well-made 57, nursed along by v- Corbin's unbeaten 24, the pair adding 69 for the third wicket while the rest of us stood about admiring their application. John Bloomfield was the pick of the attack by some distance — 2 for 23 with four maidens, not a run given away lightly, the sort of spell that makes a captain's job look easy. Trevor Back (2-39) and Paul Bowles (1-21) chipped in nicely too.

Set 143 to win, we made rather light work of it. Tim Thirkell — who now leads our 1978 run charts with 644 for the season, and looks in no mood to be caught — carried the innings with a chanceless 74 not out, while Paul Bowles, clearly not content with just bowling well, added a fine 59 of his own. The pair put on 123 for the third wicket and left the rest of the order to watch from the pavilion with cups of tea.

Home by seven wickets with overs in hand — a thoroughly good day for the club, and not a bad note to end September on.

Wed 27 Sep 1978 🧢 Midweek XIv The Mote CC · AwayformWWLDD

As we finished 155/8 after skittling for a draw at The Mote, the last of the light seeming to hold the game shut before either side could force a result — here's the report.

We lost the toss on Wednesday, and The Mote CC, sensibly enough given the way our season's going, chose to bat. It's fair to say we spent the best part of the afternoon in the field, watching John Munn compile a watchful 45, with Malcolm Bristow (31) and Ken Emmett (31 not out) chipping in either side of him as the hosts crawled to 198/5 from their 46.4 overs. Credit where it's due: Jon Harris was in fine, probing form to return 3-59, with Trevor Back (1-38) and Allan Cooper (1-48) doing their bit too — Cooper in particular bowling three tidy maidens, the sort of miserly spell that makes a captain's life easier.

Chasing 199, we made a hash of the start, losing our first wicket for just a solitary run, and things didn't improve a great deal from there — Paul Bowles and Malcolm Pay both departed without troubling the scorers, and I can offer no better account of my own dismissal than to say I too fell for nought, and the less said about that the better. It was left to Brian Piper to drag us back into contention with a really rather lovely half-century, 60 well-made runs, ably assisted by Trevor Back (23) and Allan Cooper, unbeaten on 20, as we closed on 155/8 from our 44 overs. I found some small consolation with the ball myself, picking up three wickets earlier in the day.

So, honours shared: a draw, and on this evidence a fair one, with neither side quite able to land the killer blow.

We fell to Paul CC by 8 runs at their place, chasing 159 after being put in.

Alas, Keith Miller's duck will not trouble the highlights reel. We closed on 150 for 6 from our 45, agonisingly short.

There was cause for a quiet smile amid the gloom: Thirkell brought up 4,000 career runs for the club, Piper's fifty vaulted him past Frank Taylor into 25th on the all-time run list, and Hickman's brace lifted him past Allan Cooper into 13th among the wicket-takers. Bowles and Gauna, meanwhile, continue to lead the Tour XI's run and wicket charts respectively.

Eight runs short, then — not our day, but no disgrace in it either. Heads up, and back to the nets.
