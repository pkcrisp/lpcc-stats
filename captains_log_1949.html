Sun 18 Sep 1949 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformWDLWL

Sunday 18th September, and the visitors arrived to find the coin falling kindly for once — I had no hesitation in putting us in, a decision that felt like genius by close of play and merely adequate at tea.

We crawled and occasionally sprinted to 160 for 9 off our 34 overs, the innings built almost single-handedly by John Larking, whose 52 was the sort of knock that makes a captain feel he might retire undefeated. Richard Harris chipped in a tidy 23 and G Breden 21, the two of them putting on 55 for the second wicket while the rest of us looked on approvingly from the boundary. Less approvingly received was Gordon Fenner's contribution — a duck, and the walk of shame that goes with it, though I'm told the tea urn was sympathetic.

In the field we set about defending it with more hope than method, restricting the visitors to 105 for 5 from their 34 overs. An early wicket with just 5 on the board gave us dreams of glory, but Arthur Cradduck's 33 and an unbeaten 23 from Des Latham steadied things nicely for them. T. Tubb was the pick of our bowlers with 3 for 35, backed up by George Winter and Ray Tippen, the latter positively miserly with four maidens among his figures of 1 for 17. Our wicketkeeper, it must be said, had rather a lively afternoon, shipping 13 byes.

Honours shared, then — a draw that satisfied nobody and offended nobody, which in village cricket amounts to a job well done.

Sat 17 Sep 1949 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDWDLW

The rain held off, the tea urn behaved itself, and Saturday the seventeenth of September gave us as tidy a home win as we've managed all season.

Put into the field first, we watched the visitors stumble early — 5 for 1 inside the first exchanges — before X May settled in and dragged them to a respectable-looking 119, top-scoring with 43 largely on his own. But respectable didn't last: we tore through the middle order from 65 for 4 to 73 for 7 in the blink of an eye, with X Tipple and X Little both trudging back for ducks at our hands. The afternoon belonged to Bill Hall, six wickets for 47 and six maidens to boot — a five-for and then some, thoroughly deserved — with Frank Taylor (2 for 40) and Ray Tippen (1 for 14) tidying up what was left. All out for 119 in 35.1 overs.

Chasing it down, our openers laid a proper platform, 60 for the first wicket before one fell, and Basil Sears made it look easy with a classy 54 — enough, incidentally, to nudge him past Gordon Fenner into 23rd on the club's all-time run charts. Tom Osborn (29) and Ray Tippen (20) saw us home at 121 for 4, the opposition beaten by six wickets.

A thoroughly satisfying afternoon's work — more of the same next week, please.

🗓️ 10 Sep – 16 Sep 1949 · 1949 — 1 match

Sat 10 Sep 1949 🧢 1st XIv MC Maidstone YMCA CC · AwayformWDWDL

Saturday 10th September saw us on the road to Maidstone YMCA, and for once the toss went my way — I had a good look at the strip and, in a fit of confidence I'd later question, stuck us in.

That confidence took a battering early doors. We slipped to 1-1 before anyone had really got their eye in, and the top order rather set the tone for the day — Basil Sears, Bert Buckoke and C Horton all contributing that most traditional of club cricket offerings, the golden duck. All out for 87 in 17.5 overs was a modest total, and there were one or two of us trudging back to the pavilion wondering whether we'd handed Maidstone the initiative on a plate.

We needn't have worried. With ball in hand we were a different side entirely. Maidstone wobbled from 14-1 to 21-4 and never recovered, bundled out for 51 in 18.3 overs — Smart sent back without so much as a whimper. C Horton, so cruelly done by with the bat, made handsome amends with a magnificent 6-21, while Bill Hall chipped in admirably with 4-23, including two miserly maidens that gave the batsmen nothing to work with.

A word too for the season's ledger — John Thirkell now sits fourth in the club's run charts with 128, and C Horton fifth among the wicket-takers with 8.

Won by 36 runs, two points banked, and a timely reminder that in this funny old game, batting collapses can be forgiven when your bowlers turn up in that sort of mood.

As the honorary match reporter, I'll write the report now.

From the captain's chair — Monday, 29th August 1949. We journeyed to Town Malling CC in fine spirits, and having called correctly at the toss, I had no hesitation in taking first knock. It felt the right call at the time, and I'm sticking to that story regardless of what followed.

That followed was a innings of some peril. All out 108 in 36.4 overs was, frankly, a modest return, and I confess I walked out to field with more hope than conviction.

Fortunately Frank Taylor had other ideas, steaming in for a magnificent 6 for 52 with three miserly maidens thrown in for good measure, while Don Hartridge chipped in with 4 for 43 and I found some rhythm myself with 6 wickets of my own. Town Malling fought hard — Chambers's 41 the mainstay — and slid from 69 for 3 to 72 for 6 as the pressure told, with B Simpson and Don Wooden joining the ducks' club. They fell 5 runs short, all out 103 in 30.5 overs.

A famous, nerve-shredding win by 5 runs — and a fine day too for Frank Taylor, who leapfrogged Herbert Sunley into 11th on the club's all-time wicket-takers list. Onwards.
