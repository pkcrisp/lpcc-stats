🗓️ 06 Oct – 12 Oct 1979 · 1979 — 1 match

Sat 06 Oct 1979 🧢 1st XIv BC Boughton Monchelsea CC · AwayformWWWDD

Boughton Monchelsea, Saturday last, and for once the coin fell their way — they had first knock and, more fool them, made rather a meal of it. 188 all out in 46.4 overs, which sounds respectable until you note that Nigel Thirkell was among the wickets again — 2-12, tidy as you like, part of a run that's seen him climb to second in our own wicket charts this summer. Ross Brice and Paul Bowles chipped in with two apiece, and Howard Usher went for a duck at our hands, which I mention only because he'll be mortified to see it in print. Well played anyway, Howard — could happen to anyone.

Chasing 189, we made an absolute mess of the opposition's morning by simply not getting out. Eighty-one on the board before the first wicket fell — a stand to make a captain's tea taste sweeter than usual — before Paul Bowles went on to 69, David Carden 43, and Tim Thirkell saw us home unbeaten on 28. We finished 189/4 in 35.2 overs, which is the sort of arithmetic I could get used to.

A word too for Bowles, whose two wickets that morning quietly nudged him past John Ames into third on the club's all-time list — the kind of milestone nobody notices until someone points it out, so: noticed.

Six wickets, then, and a fine one at that. Onwards indeed.

🗓️ 29 Sep – 05 Oct 1979 · 1979 — 1 match

Sat 29 Sep 1979 🧢 1st XIv Lordswood CC · HomeformDWWWD

29th September, 1979 — Lordswood CC over from across the county, and not a cloud in the sky to hide behind should things go awry, which, as you'll see, they very nearly did.

I lost the toss — I generally do — and Lordswood elected to bat, leaving us to toil away with the new ball on what I'm reliably informed was a decent surface. Jon Harris was in inspired form, tearing through their order to finish with 4-27, while Paul Bowles and John Bloomfield chipped away either side of him, Bloomfield's three maidens the very picture of a man refusing to be driven off his line. R Brown top-scored with 27, J Hendon stuck around unbeaten for the same, and Alan Pattenden made a useful 24, but for all their graft they never quite got away from us, closing on 142/8 from 51.1 overs. Poor Colin Gardener will not remember his afternoon fondly — back in the pavilion without so much as a run to his name.

Our reply, I confess, was rather more of an adventure than the scorebook suggests it should have been. A wicket down for nought had the tea urn rattling with nerves, and when 50/3 became 62/6 in the time it takes to butter a scone, three of our number — Bowles, Fermor and Iliffe — departing without scoring, I began composing my apologies for the gazette. Nigel Thirkell, though, top-scored handsomely with 39, Martin Thompson held firm unbeaten on 20, and between them they dragged us to 91/9 off our 39 overs — short of the target, but enough, mercifully, to see out the draw. Graham Cove had a torrid time behind the stumps, eight byes the cost of it, though I redeemed myself somewhat with 4 wickets of my own.

Honours shared, then, and on this evidence deservedly so — a scrap of a match that neither side quite had the nerve to win.

🗓️ 22 Sep – 28 Sep 1979 · 1979 — 2 matches

Sun 23 Sep 1979 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformDDWWW

A wobbly start with the bat, a demon spell of spin, and honours shared at the close.

Sunday 23 September brought Anchorians CC to Linton Park, and winning the toss I had no hesitation in taking first knock — proper cricket weather calls for proper decisions, and I stand by this one. Alas, we were 6 for 1 before some of the lads had found their guard, and for a while it looked like a long afternoon in the field beckoned. Credit then to David Carden, whose well-made fifty (51) was the innings' backbone, with Tim Thirkell's brisk 21 lending sturdy support as we hauled our way to 154 all out from 43 overs. As for my own contribution — nought, and the less said the better. We shall, as promised, draw a veil over it.

With the bat put away, it fell to us to defend, and defend we did. Anchorians built steadily through John Prodger's own well-made fifty (56) and a handy third-wicket stand of 50, and for a spell 117 looked a distant, worrying target. But then the floodgates opened — 108 for 5 became 109 for 8 in the blink of an eye, with G. Tappin, Jim Fish and Don Wykes all ducking out without troubling the scorers. Allan Cooper was the chief tormentor, spinning his way to a magnificent 5 for 18, with Jon Harris (3 for 58, four maidens and not an inch given) chipping in handsomely — and I confess a quiet satisfaction at my own 3 wickets too. Mike Iliffe's 16 runs nudged him past Jim Hall into 15th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, a tidy footnote to a scrappy day.

In the end, Anchorians finished 117 for 9 — short of our total, but we couldn't quite prise out that last wicket. A draw, then: honours shared, pride largely intact, and my duck already fading gently into club folklore.

Sat 22 Sep 1979 🧢 1st XIv West Farleigh CC · HomeformDDDWW

Skipper's Log, 22nd September 1979 — West Farleigh CC, at home. Lost the toss, put in to field, and I confess my heart did its usual small sink as it always does when asked to chase; but there's a certain honest pleasure in bowling first on a Saturday and seeing what the pitch has to say.

West Farleigh got off to a jittery start, three on the board and a wicket already down, which cheered us no end — only for a stand of 70 for the second wicket to remind us that cricket rarely lets you off that easily. Barry Brigden played the anchor beautifully for 80 not out, with Roger Crooks chipping in a handy 37, and between them they steered West Farleigh to 177 for 4 off their 44. Nigel Thirkell was the pick of our attack, tight as you like with six maidens and figures of 2 for 29, while John Bloomfield and Paul Bowles picked up a wicket apiece to keep things honest.

Chasing 178, we barely broke sweat. Paul Bowles played the innings of the day, 91 not out and immovable, with David Carden a fine foil on 49 and Thirkell — evidently determined to have a hand in everything — unbeaten on 31. We cantered home by nine wickets with overs to spare, and I'll admit ninety-one of my own felt rather satisfying, thank you for asking.

A thoroughly good day for the club, and with Thirkell now top of our run charts and second among the wicket-takers for the season, one begins to wonder whether the rest of us are merely there to make up the numbers.

In the end we went down by four wickets — a game we probably deserved a share of, but cricket, like the Kent weather, rarely deals in fairness. We'll dust ourselves off and be better for it next week.

Sun 09 Sep 1979 🧢 Tour XIv BC Beacon CC, Cornwall · AwayformWWWLW

Sunday the 9th of September found us on the road to Beacon CC in Cornwall, a fixture I approach each year with equal parts affection and dread, given what the pasties do to the fielding after tea. I won the toss — a rare enough event to merit its own entry in the log — and, sensing runs in the pitch, had no hesitation in batting. Mike Tritton set the tone with a fine 55, and Nigel Thirkell, ever the reliable sort, chipped in an unbeaten 47, with Paul Bowles's 28 knitting things together nicely. A commanding 100-run stand for the sixth wicket did most of the heavy lifting, and we closed on 186/6 from our forty overs feeling rather pleased with ourselves.

Defending it proved a nervier business than the scoreboard now suggests. Beacon rallied through R Tonkin's unbeaten 38 and useful contributions from D Piper and Jonathan Lean, both 28, but they never quite escaped the grip Ron Hickman had on proceedings — 3-26 with three maidens, miserly as ever. Paul Bowles and Allan Cooper chimed in with two apiece, and three Beacon batsmen departed without troubling the scorers at all. Allan did have a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, 16 byes gifted like stray sixpences, but no matter — Beacon fell for 165.

We got home by 21 runs, and Nigel Thirkell quietly brought up 7,000 career runs for the club along the way — a landmark worth toasting properly at the bar. A good day for the club, and a thoroughly satisfying one for its captain.
