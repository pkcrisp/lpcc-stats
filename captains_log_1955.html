🗓️ 24 Sep – 30 Sep 1955 · 1955 — 2 matches

Sun 25 Sep 1955 🧢 Marriedv Linton Park CC · HomeformLDDD

Sunday last, 25th September, saw Linton Park CC take the field as visitors, and when the coin span my way I did what any optimist with a soft outfield in mind would do — I stuck us in. History, and Wratten's bat, very nearly bore me out.

We were bowled out for 101 in 40.1 overs, which reads better than it felt watching the middle order come and go. Stan Wratten was the innings in miniature — 47 runs of real substance, top of the Married run charts for the season and, on this evidence, a man in form while the rest of us were still finding our guard. Tony Levick chipped in nicely with 20, I managed a workmanlike 16 of my own, and then the wheels came off in the manner club cricket does so well: Frank Brattle, Ray Tippen and Peter Weedon all walked back for ducks, three names now etched into the club's least glamorous ledger. 🦆

In the end it wasn't enough, going down by eight runs — the sort of margin that leaves you replaying every dot ball on the drive home. Not our day, by any measure.

Still, no crisis. Heads up, back to the nets, and Stan's runs are banked for the summer if not for Sunday. We live to bat again.

Sat 24 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv Town Malling CC · HomeformDWDDD

Saturday 24 September 1955, and Town Malling CC were the visitors to Linton Park. I won the toss, and there was never much doubt about my decision — up went the finger, in went the openers, and we settled to bat.

It was not, in truth, a vintage afternoon with the willow. We limped to 89/6 from 25.3 overs, a total that owed most of its respectability to John Thirkell's watchful 36 and Stan Wratten's useful 25. I contributed my own 36 to the cause, which I mention here purely for the sake of the record and not out of any vanity. Special mention, as tradition demands, to poor John Carter, who walked the walk of shame for a duck — the sort of innings that has a man studying his bootlaces rather awkwardly at tea.

Seventy-two on the board looked gettable, but our bowlers had other ideas. Town Malling were bundled out for exactly that, 72 all out in 30.4 overs, with only R- King's defiant 25 offering any real resistance. J Donovan was quite magnificent, scything through the order for 6-21 — a five-for and thoroughly deserved — while Stan Wratten, having already contributed with the bat, chipped in with 2-20 and five miserly maidens, and Bob Brigden picked up 1-18. Richard Terry, Raymond Hollands and P- Hollands all departed without troubling the scorers.

A scratchy batting display redeemed by bowling of real class. Seventeen runs the margin, and not before time — onwards we go.

🗓️ 17 Sep – 23 Sep 1955 · 1955 — 2 matches

Sun 18 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv TC Tunbridge Wells CC · HomeformWDWDD

It was 40 overs each way at Linton Park on Sunday, and after losing the toss for what feels like the third week running, I dispatched the lads into the field first — never the worst outcome when the ball's doing a bit and the tea urn's already on.

Tunbridge Wells settled in and posted 133 all out, Peter Lloyd the chief nuisance with a well-made 45, Charles Wicker (29) and Jack Hickmott (28) lending sturdy support. But just as they threatened to bat us out of the game, Gordon Fenner decided enough was enough and ran through them like a man late for his Sunday roast — 7 for 31, including ducks for James Scrace and Jim Willard, figures that will take some beating in this or any season. John Carter (1-6) and Bob Brigden (1-22, with two tidy maidens thrown in for good measure) kept things admirably mean at the other end.

Chasing 134, we made a fair fist of it without ever quite making it look easy — 130 for 7 in our forty, Ray Howard the mainstay with 32, Stan Wratten (23) and Richard Harris (20) chipping in nicely. Three runs short at the death, honours were shared, which felt about right for an afternoon of honest toil on both sides. Elsewhere, John Thirkell continues his imperious form atop the season's run charts with 793 to his name — the rest of us mere spectators.

A draw, then — no losers, but with Fenner bowling like that, no shortage of winners either.

Sat 17 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv Bearsted CC · HomeformDWDWD

I called correctly for once, and with rain never far from Kent in September, I had no hesitation in taking first knock. It didn't feel like a masterstroke early on, but John Thirkell settled things with a fine 45, Stan Wratten chipped in handsomely with 39 and Frank Brattle added a brisk 22 to see us to 145/8 from our 42. Poor C. Haylor will want to forget the whole afternoon — out for a duck, though he took it on the chin, as one must.

Defending 145 against Bearsted looked a tall order on paper, but the bowlers had other ideas. Michael Morton was magnificent, snaring 4-32, with Basil Sears (2-29) and J. Donovan (1-13, two miserly maidens) squeezing the life out of the Bearsted innings. Tony Snook's 30 and Gordon Hawkins's 27 kept the visitors interested, but 105/7 from their 43 overs was as far as they could push us, and I was glad of my own small contribution of 45 to keep the scoreboard honest.

Two landmarks to raise a glass to: Michael Morton brought up his 100th career wicket, and Basil Sears's brace edged him past R.W. Mitchell into 24th on the club's all-time list — company he can be proud to keep. Thirkell continues to lead our run charts for the season with 780, and Morton now sits third among the wicket-takers with 35.

A draw, then — honours shared, tea well earned, and nobody quite sure whether to call it a triumph of defiance or a missed opportunity. I shall call it the former.

Sun 11 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformDDWDW

It was another of those Sundays that reminded us why we love this maddening game. We lost the toss, were sent out to field, and watched Dummy Site build a respectable 192/6 from their 54 overs, with H. Doust playing the innings of the day — a well-crafted 74 — ably assisted by K. Walker (39) and A. Lawson (37). Credit where it's due: Len Conway bowled beautifully for 2-35 with three miserly maidens, while Bob Brigden (2-45) and John Thirkell (1-30) kept things honest at the other end.

Chasing 193, we made a fair fist of it, closing on 166/7 from our 36 overs. John Thirkell top-scored with a fine 59, well supported by Stan Wratten (32) and Tony Levick (22) — and yes, that 59 was mine, and I shan't pretend I wasn't rather pleased with it.

Two milestones worth toasting in the clubhouse this week: Stan brought up 3,000 career runs for the club, a landmark built one gritty innings at a time, and John's solitary wicket nudged him past Jack Fryer into 8th on our all-time wicket-takers list — and he now leads the 1955 first-team run charts with 735 for the season, a fact he will doubtless remind us of at every opportunity.

In the end neither side could force it, and honours were shared. A draw, then — honest toil, no shame, and tea was rather good.

Sat 10 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv DS Dummy Site · HomeformWDDWD

From the captain's chair, Saturday 10 September 1955 — we hosted Dummy Site, and having lost the toss, found ourselves invited to bowl first, which around here always feels less like an invitation and more like a summons.

They totted up 147 all out in 46.2 overs, Kingston the chief architect with 42, R. Howard and Roberts chipping in with 30 and 25 respectively. It might have been a good deal worse had Bob Brigden not decided to turn the ball square all afternoon, finishing with the wonderful figures of 5-29, four of them maidens — miserly barely covers it. J Donovan and Michael Morton mopped up a wicket apiece, and between them we kept Dummy Site to a total that felt gettable.

Getting it, mind, was another matter. We reached 148 for 7 in 31.4 overs, C. Martin and Deer having gone quietly early on without troubling the scorers — ducks, the pair of them, sympathies extended. Bob Arnold led from the front with a fine 38, John Thirkell added 31 and Tom Osborn 22, while John "-'s Taylor and Michael Morton both departed for golden ducks of their own. I managed to contribute 31 myself, which I shall modestly describe as timely rather than pretty.

Gordon Fenner's 22 nudged him past Bob Arnold to 17th on the club's all-time run-scorers list, while John Thirkell continues to lead the 1955 run charts and John "-'s Taylor sits second among the season's wicket-takers. A fine win by three wickets — onwards we go.

Sat 03 Sep 1955 🧢 1st XIv YC Yalding CC · AwayformWLWDD

1955, Yalding CC away. We lost the toss and were asked to bowl first, which on a fine September afternoon felt less like misfortune and more like Yalding sparing us the indignity of batting first ourselves. Yalding were dismissed for 91 in 42.4 overs, a total that flattered them somewhat given the treatment meted out by John "--'s" Taylor, whose 7-41 (eight maidens, no less — the man was positively stingy) was as fine a spell as the gazette will report all season. John Thirkell chipped in handsomely with 3-35, R. Woollett departed for a duck at our hands, and only a- Stickings, with 33, offered any real resistance for the home side.

Our reply was, in the finest traditions of Linton Park, rather more eventful than the situation required. Chasing 92 to win, we contrived to make hard work of it, stumbling to 98/6 from 32 overs before finally getting over the line — John Taylor again to the fore with 22, proving he's rather good at this game in both disciplines. Your skipper, meanwhile, enjoyed a happy afternoon of his own with three wickets, which I shall modestly mention and then move swiftly on from.

Four wickets the margin, two points the reward, and thoroughly earned despite our best efforts to make a nonsense of it. With Thirkell now leading the 1st XI run charts on 631 and Taylor sitting second in the wickets with 39, the season has rather the look of a two-man band carrying the rest of us along — and long may they keep playing.

🗓️ 27 Aug – 02 Sep 1955 · 1955 — 1 match

Sun 28 Aug 1955 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformWWLWD

It was Sunday, 28 August 1955, and the sun found us at home with our whites on and the tea urn warming — happy days.

We took first use of the ball and the visitors made a fair fist of it, closing on 189 for 9 from their 46 overs. R Aiston led the charge with a well-crafted 52, and got good company from E Lawrence (36) and J Basten (32). But the story of the innings belonged to John Ames, who bowled with real purpose to return 5 for 76 — a five-for thoroughly earned and thoroughly deserved. Tony Tipples (2-33) and Tony Snell (1-30, with two tidy maidens to his name) kept things honest at the other end.

In reply we made 140 for 7 from our 39, William Buck the pick of the willow with 42, backed up nicely by Tom Osborn's 24 and an unbeaten 22 from Tony Levick. Less gloriously, Tony Tipples, John Ames and Peter Weedon all took the long walk back for nought — the Duck Club swells its ranks again. Your correspondent must confess to joining that unhappy list with the bat, though I found some consolation with the ball, picking up five wickets of my own.

One shining moment amid it all: Tom Osborn brought up 1,000 career runs for the club, a landmark thoroughly deserved and warmly applauded by all.

Honours ended shared, a draw when neither side could quite force the door open — and on a day like this, with milestones reached and ducks laid, I'll happily settle for that.

We lost the toss on Saturday and were duly sent in to chase — never our preferred order of business, but there we are. We scrambled home by three wickets. Untidy in places, occasionally alarming, but a win is a win, and on a warm August Saturday, that will do very nicely indeed.

The lads and I gathered on a fine Sunday, 14th August, with Dummy Site down to visit Linton Park. I won the toss and, fancying the wicket would only get harder to bat on as the day wore on, had no hesitation in sending us in first.

Two wickets the margin, and a long, thoughtful drive home for me. Not our day, but heads up - it's back to the nets, and there's a season yet to be won.

Sun 07 Aug 1955 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformDWLLW

Sunday the 7th of August, and Anchorians CC came to Linton Park with the sun out and, rather less welcome, the toss lost — we were put in the field. Not that we minded much, as it turned out. They wobbled from the off, one down without a run on the board, and never quite found their feet, subsiding from a hopeful-ish 89-7 to 102 all out in 55.4 overs. Only P- Clarke, with a battling 32, threatened to give the innings any shape. John Ames was the chief tormentor, prising out four for 22 with eight overs of maidens that must have felt like a small siege, Tony Snell chipped in with four for 33 of his own — enough, we're told, to nudge him past Ernest Cruttenden into 12th on the club's all-time list — and Bob Brigden took one for 27 for good measure. S- Harden, J- Harris and B Mitchell all departed without so much as troubling the scorers.

Set 103 to win, we made harder work of it than the target deserved, losing our own opener without a run before Tony Levick (42) and Ray Howard (41 not out) put together 76 for the second wicket and steadied the ship. William Buck and John Thirkell both walked for nought, I contributed my customary duck, and eight byes suggested the wicketkeeping was having as testing a day as anyone. Still, 105 for 4 in 28.3 overs got us home with six wickets to spare — a fine win, however scruffily assembled, and one we'll happily take.

🗓️ 23 Jul – 29 Jul 1955 · 1955 — 1 match

Sun 24 Jul 1955 🧢 1st XIv FC Folkestone CC · AwayformDDDDW

Sunday 24 July 1955 saw us pack the kit bags for Folkestone, hopes modest, sun (mercifully) cooperative. I lost the toss — no great surprise, my coin-calling remains as reliable as an English summer — and we were duly sent out to field first, which at least spared us the indignity of batting on a fresh pitch with cold hands.
