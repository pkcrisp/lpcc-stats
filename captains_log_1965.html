🗓️ 25 Sep – 01 Oct 1965 · 1965 — 2 matches

Sun 26 Sep 1965 🧢 1st XIv Linton Park CC · HomeformDWLDD

Sunday the twenty-sixth of September, and Linton Park CC came calling. I won the toss and had no hesitation in taking first knock — the sort of decision that looks either shrewd or reckless depending entirely on what follows, and I'm pleased to report it fell, on balance, into the former category.

We laboured to 136 for 8 off our thirty-nine overs, which on paper looks a modest total and in practice felt like one too, though not without its merits. John Thirkell batted with real class for an unbeaten 63, the innings' one point of genuine certainty, and found a willing ally in Stan Wratten, whose 22 gave the total some much-needed backbone. Then, as so often happens in this maddening game, the wheels came off with startling efficiency — 126 for 5 became 136 for 8 almost before anyone had settled back into their deckchair. Poor Roy Clarke will not thank me for mentioning his duck, but the scorebook is unforgiving and so, occasionally, must I be.

Elsewhere in the ledger, John Thirkell continues to lead the First XI's run charts for the season with 863 to his name, while Robert Chance heads the Second XI's with 31 — every column matters when the tea urn's on the line.

In the end neither side could force the issue, and honours were shared. A draw, then — not the stuff of legend, but a fair reflection of an afternoon where good batting met better bowling, and Sunday tea was, as ever, the true victor.

Sat 25 Sep 1965 🧢 1st XIv ? · HomeformDDWLD

Saturday 25th September saw us welcome the visitors for what turned out to be a proper old-fashioned tussle, honours shared at the close. Put in first with the ball, we made early inroads with just 5 on the board and dared to dream, only for a stubborn 63-run stand at the fifth wicket to drag things back the visitors' way. Dick's four-for also nudged him past Jack Fryer into 17th on the club's all-time wicket-takers list, which is the sort of thing that makes the teas taste sweeter.

A draw, then, when neither side could quite land the killer blow. Not a classic, but an honest one — and with John Thirkell and Terry Whittaker still purring along at the top of this season's charts, there's plenty yet to play for.

🗓️ 18 Sep – 24 Sep 1965 · 1965 — 2 matches

Sun 19 Sep 1965 🧢 1st XIv AC Anchorians CC · HomeformLDDWL

Anchorians came to Linton Park on the 19th of September and, having called correctly at the toss, I fancied a bat — and for once the skipper's hunch didn't let him down entirely, even if 3-1 had the look of a side about to make a fool of its captain. William Buck's 33 was the innings' backbone, with John Ames and Frank Brattle chipping in 26 apiece to drag us to a respectable if unspectacular 139/9 from our 44.5 overs. Special mention must go to Stan Wratten, who contributed a duck of such quality it deserves its own line in the ledger.

Defending it, the lads turned in the sort of bowling display that makes all those Sunday nets worthwhile. Alan Tucker was quite magnificent — 4-18 with seven maidens, miserly to the point of parsimony — well supported by Terry Whittaker's 3-17 and a wicket from John Ames. Anchorians found themselves 3-1 and then, extraordinarily, 4-4, and three of their number trudged back without so much as a run to show for it. J- Harris's 27 and Jim Fish's 23 were the only real resistance, and Anchorians subsided to 90/9. Poor Robert Chance had a torrid afternoon behind the stumps, twelve byes leaking away, while I did my modest bit with 26 of my own.

Amid it all, Terry Whittaker and Dick Langridge both climbed the club's all-time charts — quiet milestones on a day of honest toil. In the end neither side could force the issue, and honours were shared. A draw, then — but not, I think, an unsatisfying one.

Sat 18 Sep 1965 🧢 1st XIv BC Bowaters CC · AwayformLLDDW

From the captain's chair — Saturday 18 September 1965, and the road took us to Bowaters CC in what I confess was one of those afternoons that tests a man's love of the game.

Winning the toss, I did the sensible thing and put us in, only for the sensible thing to look rather foolish by close of play. We laboured to 81 all out in 43.1 overs, with Fred Greenfield the one man to bring any authority to proceedings — 33 well-made runs while all about him lost their heads. And lose them we did: 77 for 7 became 81 for 10 with barely time to draw breath, Colin Eldred and D. Ibbotson both departing for golden ducks, as if the pitch had been mined.

Eighty-one was never going to trouble anybody, and so it proved, though not without a fight from the ball. Gordon Fenner bowled beautifully for 3-25, with Terry Whittaker miserly as ever — two maidens and 1-14 — and between them they at least made Bowaters work, John Evans sent back without so much as a nudge. But Robin Scott's unbeaten 36 saw them safely home at 85 for 4 from 24 overs, a six-wicket win that flattered nobody unduly. I chipped in with 18, for what little comfort that offered.

Elsewhere in the season, Frank Brattle continues his steady climb up the run charts (358, fifth in the 1st XI), and Terry Whittaker leads the wicket-takers with 55 — proof, if any were needed, that not everything about our campaign is held together with string. As for Saturday: a chastening one, but we'll be better for it next week.

Sunday 12 September 1965, and the road took us out to Staplehurst. I called correctly at the toss and, with barely a flicker of hesitation, sent us in — a decision that looked rather braver in hindsight than it felt at the time.

Thirkell's 21 the top score amid a collapse of our making — 26 for 1 crumbled to 35 for 4, with R. Newick and L. Mitchell both ducking out for nothing. The skipper wasn't idle either, with five wickets of his own to show for the afternoon.

Frank Brattle continues his quiet season, now 5th in the run charts with 354, while Langridge sits 3rd among the wicket-takers with 43. We got home by 27 runs — scrappy, nervy, thoroughly typical, and thoroughly deserved.

Sun 05 Sep 1965 🧢 1st XIv HC Harrietsham CC · HomeformWLLLD

Sunday the 5th of September, and Harrietsham arrived to find the Linton Park coin obliging me for once — heads it was, and, feeling unusually decisive for a man who normally can't choose a tea order, I sent us in.

What followed was fifteen overs of watchful, faintly nervous cricket, the sort where every run feels earned through negotiation rather than conquest. We crept to 31 for the loss of one, the scoreboard ticking over with the enthusiasm of a kettle that can't quite decide whether to boil. Wickets stayed largely intact, ambition rather less so, and by the close both sides seemed to have silently agreed that discretion was the better part of valour.

In the end neither team could summon the wit, the weather, or perhaps the will to force a result, and honours were shared — a draw that satisfied no one and offended no one, which in village cricket terms is practically a triumph of diplomacy. There was no real cut and thrust, more a gentle handshake of an afternoon, the kind where the tea interval threatens to be the highlight and very nearly is.

Still, we came, we batted, we didn't lose — and against Harrietsham, on a day like this, I'll happily take a draw and a decent slice of cake as a job honourably done.

Poor J Beesley won't remember his golden duck fondly. My own contribution behind the stumps was rather less becoming — eight byes conceded is not a figure I'll be framing — and I added a modest 21 with the bat for good measure.

St Lukes closed on 162 for 7, honours shared. A draw, in the end, felt about right: two sides that gave as good as they got, and neither quite good enough to finish the job.

Poor Richard Ashby had a torrid time behind the stumps too, 11 byes going begging. All out for 78, we fell 129 runs short.

Not our day. Heads up, and back to the nets.
